# Chapter One: The Arriving

---

Tomas Herdsson woke before sunrise on his sixteenth birthday, and the first thing he felt was the cold.

Not the ordinary cold of a Veridale spring morning — the seep-through-wool, bite-at-the-ankles cold he'd known every day of his life. This was different. This was a cold that came from inside, spreading outward from the base of his skull like winter water poured down his spine. He opened his mouth to call for his father, but no sound came out. The world had gone silent. The bleating of the sheep in the paddock, the wind through the thatch, his own heartbeat — all of it, gone.

Then he was standing in the corner of the room, watching himself lie in bed.

*That's me*, he thought, with the strange calm of someone who had not yet understood that the rules had changed. *I look small.*

He didn't. Tomas Herdsson at sixteen was built like the sheep he'd spent his life guarding — broad through the shoulders, solid in the chest, the kind of body that suggested it had been designed to stand in front of things and refuse to move. But from outside himself, watching the rise and fall of his own breathing, he felt like a child playing at being a man.

And then he saw it.

A thread of light, woven through his chest, branching outward into patterns he had no name for. It pulsed once — a heartbeat not his own — and Tomas understood, in the way that Understanding came during awakening, exactly what he was.

*Totemic Sentinel.*

The words arrived fully formed, as if they had always been there and he had simply never had cause to read them. Below them, an image: a ram. Not the sheep of his father's flock — soft-eyed, wool-heavy, bred for milk and meat — but something older. Horns curved like a question asked before language existed. Eyes that had watched wolves come and wolves go.

The vision dissolved. Tomas returned to his body with a gasp that tasted like copper. The bleating of the sheep returned. The wind. His heartbeat.

"Da," he said, and this time his voice worked. "Da, I'm awake."

---

Two hundred miles east, in a room that smelled of distilled nightshade and old money, Flynn Wintersbloom was not waking up at all.

He had been awake for hours. He had been awake, in fact, since three in the morning, when the house staff had begun preparing the east parlour for what was supposed to be a celebration. Flynn could hear them through three walls and a floor — the clink of good silver, the rustle of linen, the carefully modulated voices of servants who had been trained not to sound excited. The Wintersbloom family did not get excited. The Wintersbloom family *anticipated*. There was a difference, and it was measured in the angle of a chin.

Flynn sat on the edge of his bed, still in his nightclothes, and waited for his soul to tell him what it was going to be.

His mother was an Apothecarian. His father was an Apothecarian. His grandfather had been Arch-Apothecarian of Miravale for forty-seven years. The Wintersbloom name appeared in seventeen different potion patents, including the standard field-stabilization brew used by every Guild medic between here and the Thornwood frontier. Flynn had been raised in a laboratory. He had learned titration before he learned to read. He would be an Apothecarian, because Wintersblooms were always Apothecarians, because that was how blood worked, because that was how *everything* worked.

At eight-fifteen in the morning, exactly sixteen years after his first breath, Flynn Wintersbloom discovered that everything did not, in fact, work that way.

The heat hit him like a fever breaking in reverse — a sudden, violent compression at the base of his skull, and then the world vanished. No sound. No scent. No weight of the bed beneath him. He was outside himself, looking down at a thin boy with too-sharp features and hands that had never held anything heavier than a stirring rod, and the mana-path through his soul was not the steady, methodical lattice of an Apothecarian.

It was a kaleidoscope.

Fractured light, spinning, refusing to settle. Patterns that formed for half a heartbeat and then shattered into something new. There was no order to it — there was *motion*. Constant, unpredictable, almost mocking. And beneath the chaos, a name:

*Cantrip Jester.*

Flynn Wintersbloom, heir to the Wintersbloom Apothecarian dynasty, was a Stage Class.

He returned to his body and said nothing for a very long time. Downstairs, the servants had stopped moving. They were waiting. The whole house was waiting. Flynn stared at his hands — the hands that would never hold a stabilization brew, never sign a patent, never carry the weight of seventeen ancestral achievements — and felt, for the first time in his life, something that was not anticipation.

Relief.

---

The carriage that carried Flynn to PactSpire was Wintersbloom lacquer-black, pulled by four Rune-Touched horses whose shoes struck sparks from the Old North Road. It was the kind of carriage that made farmers step off the road and tug their forelocks. It was the kind of carriage that announced, before its passenger ever spoke a word, that someone important was arriving.

Flynn sat in the back, watching the farmland roll past, and tried to decide whether he was supposed to feel guilty.

His father had not spoken to him since the awakening. Not a word. Lord Castellan Wintersbloom had simply looked at his son — one long, unreadable look — and then turned and walked into his study and closed the door. That had been three days ago. The door had not opened since. Flynn's mother had arranged the carriage, the luggage, the tuition payment, all without once meeting his eyes. The message was clear: *You are still a Wintersbloom. You will attend PactSpire. After that, we will see.*

The horses slowed. Flynn looked up.

PactSpire rose from the valley floor like something that had grown rather than been built. Five concentric rings of stone and wood and living green, stacked in ascending terraces toward a central tower that pierced the low cloud. The architecture shifted as he watched — a window blinked from one floor to another, a section of wall breathed inward as if adjusting its posture, a staircase reconfigured itself from spiral to straight. The campus was alive. Flynn had read about it, of course. Everyone had. But reading that a building could move and watching it *turn to look at you* were not the same thing.

"Saints," the driver muttered. "It's looking at us."

"Don't be absurd," Flynn heard himself say, in a voice he barely recognized — clipped, aristocratic, the voice of someone who had been raised to find nothing impressive. "It's looking at the horses."

---

Tomas arrived on foot, three hours later, with dirt on his boots and a ram-shaped shadow that walked beside him.

He had said goodbye to his father at the Veridale crossroads. His father had given him a sheepskin vest — the one Tomas's mother had made, before she died — and a small pouch of dried mutton, and a piece of advice that Tomas was still trying to parse.

"If it protects you," his father had said, nodding at the spectral ram, "you protect it back. That's the whole thing. Don't let anyone tell you different."

Tomas had nodded. He had not asked what his father meant, because his father was not the kind of man who explained things twice, and because Tomas thought he might understand it later, when it mattered. That was how his father taught: in pieces that didn't fit together until you needed them to.

Now, standing at the base of the Farm Ring — the outermost ring of PactSpire, where the agricultural apprentices worked and lived — Tomas understood that he had absolutely no idea what he was doing.

The ram stood at his side, head high, nostrils flaring at the scent of the campus. It was taller than it had been that first morning, Tomas noticed. Or perhaps it had always been that tall, and he simply hadn't known how to measure it. The totem was part of him in a way he couldn't explain — not a separate creature, exactly, but not entirely himself either. It moved when he *wanted* it to move, without the middle step of deciding. It was, he was beginning to suspect, the version of him that knew what to do.

"Right," Tomas said, to the ram. "Let's go be impressive."

The ram did not reply. The ram did not need to. They both knew Tomas was not going to be impressive.

---

The Apprentice Registry was housed in a building that had, according to the carved lintel above its door, once been a chapel. The old altar had been repurposed as a registration desk. Behind it sat a woman with grey hair pulled tight enough to stretch the skin at her temples and an expression that suggested she had been dealing with new students for approximately three hundred years and was not planning to enjoy it now.

"Name," she said, without looking up.

"Tomas Herdsson."

"Kingdom."

"Veridale."

"Class."

"Totemic Sentinel."

The woman's pen stopped. She looked up — really looked, for the first time — and her eyes went past Tomas to the ram standing at his shoulder.

"We don't get many Sword Classes in the Farm Ring," she said. "Usually they come in through the Training Ring entrance. All that shouting."

"I didn't know there were different entrances."

"There aren't, officially." She made a note in her ledger. "But the campus has opinions. The fact that it let you in through the Farm Ring means something." She looked at the ram again. "It means you belong to the land more than the blade. Don't forget that."

Tomas didn't know what to say to that, so he said nothing. The ram flicked one ear. The woman handed him a small copper key — warm to the touch, as if it had been sitting in sunlight — and a folded map that Tomas suspected would be useless by tomorrow.

"Dormitory Ring, Hall Verdant, room forty-seven. The campus will find you when it wants you."

---

Flynn's registration took thirty seconds and required him to speak exactly two words: his name.

"That's fine," the registrar — a different woman, in a different building, in the much cleaner and better-lit section of the Classroom Ring — said, already writing. "Lord Wintersbloom's son. We've been expecting you. You'll be in Hall Clarion, east wing. Best views on campus. Your father's arrangements."

"My father's arrangements," Flynn repeated.

"Yes." The registrar finally looked up. Something flickered in her expression — curiosity, perhaps, or sympathy. "Stage Division, I'm told. That's... unexpected."

"Is it?" Flynn said, in the voice that wasn't quite his.

The registrar decided not to answer that. She handed him a silver key and a map printed on paper that smelled faintly of mint. "The campus will find you when it wants you," she said, which was apparently something everyone said here.

---

They met in the Grand Hall, three hours before the Pact-Planting ceremony, because the campus had decided they should.

It was not an accident. The living architecture of PactSpire did not do accidents. Later, some of the older students would explain that the campus had a sense of narrative — that it arranged coincidences the way a gardener arranged flowers, placing certain colors beside each other to see what grew. At the time, neither Tomas nor Flynn understood this. All they understood was that they had both been sent to collect their ceremonial robes from the same supply closet at the same time, and that the door had locked behind them.

"Did you just lock us in?" Flynn asked the door.

The door did not answer. Doors here rarely did.

"It's the campus," Tomas said, from behind him. "The registrar said it has opinions."

Flynn turned. The boy standing in the corner of the supply closet was at least half a head taller than him and built like something that had been designed to withstand weather. Dirt on his boots. A sheepskin vest that had seen better decades. And behind him — no, *beside* him, as if it had always been there — a spectral ram, horned and watchful, its form flickering at the edges like heat haze over summer stone.

"A Sword Class," Flynn said. "In a supply closet. With me."

"Looks that way."

"I'm a Stage Class."

"I know." Tomas's eyes flicked to Flynn's hands — the uncalloused fingers, the immaculate cuffs. "You look like one."

It was not an insult, exactly, but it was not a compliment either. Flynn felt something tighten in his chest — the old instinct, the Wintersbloom instinct, to establish superiority before anyone had the chance to question it.

"And you look like you've been sleeping in a barn."

"I have been sleeping in a barn," Tomas said. "For sixteen years. It's very comfortable."

The ram made a sound — not quite a bleat, not quite a growl — and the supply closet felt suddenly very small.

Flynn opened his mouth to say something cutting, something that would put this farm boy in his proper place, and then stopped. Because he could feel it. Something at the edge of his perception, something his new Cantrip Jester senses were only beginning to learn how to read. The ram was not just standing beside Tomas. The ram was *watching* Tomas. And Tomas, without looking, without seeming to notice he was doing it, had positioned himself slightly in front of Flynn. Between Flynn and the door. Between Flynn and whatever might come through it.

Protective instinct. It wasn't a choice. It was what the Class did.

"Your totem," Flynn said, more quietly. "It's not just a weapon, is it."

"No," Tomas said. "It's not."

They stood in silence for a moment. The ram watched Flynn. Flynn watched the ram. Tomas watched the floor, because Tomas was still learning how to be looked at.

The door unlocked itself with a quiet click.

"We should get the robes," Tomas said.

"We should," Flynn agreed.

Neither of them moved for another five seconds.

---

That evening, standing in the Grand Hall under a sky that was and was not the real sky — the ceiling of the Spire could show any weather it pleased, and tonight it had chosen a clear Veridale spring, full of stars Tomas recognized — seven hundred new students waited to swear their first oath.

Pact-Planting. The Apprentice Vow. The words were personal; there was no script. Each student would walk to the center of the Hall, stand before their Division head, and speak whatever promise their soul could offer. The Pact Library's book would write the name, or it wouldn't. The campus would accept, or it wouldn't. Everyone knew that one or two students would be asked to leave before dawn. Everyone knew that no one would speak of them afterward.

Tomas watched Flynn walk to the center of the Hall when the Stage Division was called. The Wintersbloom heir moved like someone who had been trained to walk in front of crowds but had never learned to enjoy it. He stood before Maestro Corvin Stagewright — a tall, angular man with hands that never stopped moving — and spoke his vow in a voice that carried to every corner of the Hall.

It was beautiful. It was also, Tomas realized with a strange pang of recognition, entirely artificial. A performance. Flynn was performing sincerity, because Flynn didn't know how to be sincere in public, because sincerity was not something the Wintersbloom family did.

The Pact Library's book wrote his name. The floor beneath him warmed. The campus accepted.

Flynn walked back to his seat with his jaw set and his eyes fixed on the floor, and Tomas understood something he hadn't expected to understand: Flynn Wintersbloom was just as lost here as he was.

---

When the Sword Division was called, Tomas stood.

The ram rose beside him.

He walked to the center of the Hall. The Grandmaster of the Sword Division was a woman named Kestrel Ashford — compact, grey-haired, with a scar that ran from her left temple to her jaw and eyes that had seen things Tomas couldn't imagine. She looked at the ram, then at Tomas, and something in her expression shifted. Not approval. Not quite. Recognition.

"You're Veridale," she said, quietly enough that only Tomas could hear.

"Yes."

"My grandmother was Veridale. Sheep country."

"Mine too," Tomas said. "I mean — I am. Sheep country."

Grandmaster Ashford almost smiled. "Then you know how to stand your ground. Good. You'll need it."

Tomas looked at the Hall — the hundreds of faces, the living stone beneath his feet, the stars that were not stars — and tried to find words. The vow was supposed to be personal. The vow was supposed to be true. Tomas had never been good at words, but he had spent sixteen years being good at one thing: standing in front of things and refusing to move.

"I don't know much," he said, and his voice was rougher than he'd meant it to be, "but I know how to stay. I know how to take a hit. I know how to make sure the things behind me don't have to. If that's what the Sword Division wants, then that's what I'm offering. If it's not, then I don't know what else I have."

Silence. The ram stood motionless at his side.

The Pact Library's book wrote his name. The stone beneath his feet grew so warm he could feel it through his boots. The campus accepted.

And somewhere in the Hall, in the Stage Division section, Flynn Wintersbloom — who had given the most beautiful vow of the evening and felt nothing — watched a farm boy stumble through the truth and felt something crack open in his chest that he didn't have a name for yet.

---

That night, in Hall Verdant, room forty-seven, Tomas lay in a bed that was not his and listened to the building breathe. The walls expanded and contracted with a rhythm like distant surf. The floor hummed at a frequency just below hearing. The ram stood in the corner, head lowered, watching the door.

Tomorrow, training would begin. Tomorrow, he would meet the rest of his cohort — the other six students the campus had decided belonged together. Tomorrow, he would learn what it meant to be a Totemic Sentinel in a world that didn't quite know what to do with one.

But tonight, Tomas Herdsson — sheep farmer, Veridale son, protector of things that couldn't protect themselves — closed his eyes and let the living campus sing him to sleep.

The ram watched the door.

In Hall Clarion, three rings away, Flynn Wintersbloom did not sleep at all. He sat at his window — the best view on campus, as his father had arranged — and watched the Fracture Line glow faintly in the central courtyard. The crack that ran through PactSpire like the crack that ran through the world itself, a remnant of the emperor's betrayal, a wound that had never healed.

His father had not written. His mother had not written. He was a Wintersbloom in a Stage Division, a Cantrip Jester in a family of Apothecarians, a performer who had never performed anything real in his life.

But tonight, in the Grand Hall, he had watched a boy who couldn't lie tell the truth, and something about that truth had lodged under his skin like a splinter.

*If it protects you, you protect it back.*

Flynn didn't know Tomas had been told those words. He didn't know they were the last advice a sheep farmer had given his son. But as he watched the Fracture Line pulse with a light that was not quite moonlight, Flynn Wintersbloom — heir to nothing he wanted, holder of a Class he didn't understand — made a decision.

Tomorrow, he would find out who Tomas Herdsson was.

Tomorrow, everything would begin.
