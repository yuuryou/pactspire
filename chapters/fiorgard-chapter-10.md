# Chapter Ten: The Letter

The letter arrived on the first day of spring.

It came by Guild courier — not the campus message system, which would have been normal, but a formal Apothecarian Guild dispatch, sealed with the Wintersbloom crest and addressed in a hand that Flynn recognized before he touched it. His mother's hand. But the seal was his father's.

The courier delivered it during morning formation. Flynn took the envelope without a word, slipped it into his training tunic, and completed the entire Combat Fundamentals session — a two-hour drill on aegis reinforcement, Tomas pushing the ram to its limits while Flynn fed the sphere with his now-practiced mana flow — without mentioning it.

Tomas felt it anyway.

The bond between them had grown more sensitive since the solstice. Not invasive — Tomas could still maintain his own emotional space, and Flynn could still bolt his own doors — but the background hum of the provisional pact was stronger now. Clearer. When Flynn was upset, Tomas knew. When Tomas was worried, Flynn felt it. They had learned to live with the resonance, to accept it as the price of the bond, but there were moments — like this one — when it was less a background hum and more a steady, insistent pulse.

"Shepherd," Flynn said, when the drill ended. "You're staring."

"You have a letter."

"I'm aware."

"You haven't opened it."

"I'm aware of that too."

They walked to the edge of the Training Ring, where the ram was recovering its form after the exertion of the drill. Tomas waited. He had learned, over the past months, that Flynn would talk when he was ready and not before. Pushing him only made him perform.

"It's from my father," Flynn said. "My mother wrote the address, but the seal is his. He hasn't written to me directly since the Apprentice Vow. This is the first time." He paused. "And I don't want to open it."

"Because you know what it says?"

"Because I'm afraid of what it says."

They sat on the stone bench at the edge of the Ring. The spring morning was bright — the first true warmth after the long winter — and the campus was responding to the season with its usual enthusiasm. Flowers Tomas didn't recognize were blooming along the pathway. The Fracture Line was pulsing with a soft, gold-tinged light. Somewhere near the Grand Hall, a second-year was practicing a Terra-Mage cultivation technique, and the ground was humming with the particular vibration of growing things.

"I'll stay," Tomas said.

"You don't have to."

"I know."

Flynn opened the letter.

The bond between them went sharp — a spike of emotion so strong that Tomas felt it as a physical sensation. Not pain. Something worse. The particular, piercing ache of a wound that had been expected but not prepared for. Flynn didn't cry. His expression didn't change. He read the letter once, then again, his hands perfectly steady, his Quick-Cast sparking so violently that the sparks were leaving scorch marks on the bench.

"Read it," Flynn said.

"It's private."

"I want you to read it."

Tomas took the letter.

*Flynn,*

*Your mother writes this at my request. She will not tell you what I am about to say, because she loves you too much to be honest.*

*The Alchemical-Stage duel was a disgrace. Not because you lost — because you won. You used my training against me. You used the knowledge I spent years imparting to you to publicly humiliate the Guild that I represent. You turned my investment into a weapon and pointed it at my own colleagues.*

*I have spent seventeen years preparing for you to inherit the Wintersbloom name and the Apothecarian practice that has sustained our line for four centuries. You have now made it clear — publicly, emphatically, irrevocably — that you will not be that heir. I accept this. I accept that the Wintersbloom name will die with you.*

*But I will not subsidize its death. As of this letter, the Wintersbloom account at the PactSpire bursary is closed. Your tuition, your supplies, your living expenses — none of these will come from the family name. The Guild's scholarship for Apothecarian heirs has been transferred to Gryphon Vale, who, despite his defeat, remains a loyal member of the Guild. Your mother has no independent account. There is no other family.*

*You have chosen the Cantrip Jester's path. Live on it.*

*Your father,*
*Oswald Wintersbloom*

Tomas read the letter twice. The ram, which had been resting, stood and pressed its head against his leg — a gesture of comfort it had never made before.

"He's cutting you off," Tomas said.

"Yes."

"Completely. No tuition. No living expenses."

"Yes."

"Can the campus—"

"No. PactSpire's charter allows withdrawal of family sponsorship for any reason. There's no appeal process. The Masters can't override a Guild seal."

Tomas folded the letter carefully, precisely, the way someone else might have crumpled it. "What are you going to do?"

Flynn was silent for a long moment. The Quick-Cast sparks were still dancing, but slower now — not anxiety, Tomas realized. Fury. The cold, quiet fury of someone who had finally been pushed past the point of performance.

"I'm going to prove him wrong," Flynn said. "Not by fighting. Not by arguing. By succeeding. By staying here, in this Class, in this cohort, and becoming so good that the Apothecarian Guild has to admit I made the right choice. That's what he can't stand — not that I'm a Cantrip Jester, but that I might be a *good* one. That I might be better at this than I ever could have been at alchemy."

"Then we need a plan."

Flynn looked at him. "We?"

"The cohort. All of us. We've been training together for two terms. We're a unit. One of our own is in trouble." Tomas's voice was steady, the way it always was when he had decided something. "We don't abandon unit members."

"You sound like Renn."

"Renn is frequently right."

"That's the most terrifying thing you've ever said."

---

The cohort met that evening in Hall Verdant.

The mood was different from the Alchemical-Stage duel planning session. That had been strategic — a problem to solve, an opponent to outmaneuver. This was personal. Flynn was one of them. Flynn was being attacked. And the cohort, which had spent a full academic year learning to fight together, knew how to respond to an attack.

"The Wintersbloom account," Ivy said, reading the letter one more time, "was worth approximately three thousand marks per term. That's not an impossible sum. PactSpire's work-study program covers half of it for qualifying students. The Guild scholarship fund for independent Class studies —" She flipped through her notebook. "— covers another five hundred marks. That leaves about a thousand marks per term to find."

"A thousand marks is still a thousand marks," Kael said.

"Yes. But it's divisible." Ivy closed her notebook with a snap. "If the six of us each contribute part of our own allowances, we can cover the gap. I have a Forge Division research stipend that I'm not fully using. Sera has her frontier settlement grant. Renn's family—"

"—will contribute," Renn said, before Ivy could finish. "The Drakengard line remembers favors. Flynn's father embarrassed the Apothecarian Guild's reputation. That makes him an enemy of anyone who values guild honor. The Drakengard have a long memory for enemies."

"This isn't about enemies," Tomas said. "This is about Flynn."

"The two are frequently the same thing."

Wren spoke from their corner, their voice quiet as always: "There's also the campus. The Spire provides for its own. The work-study program is part of it, but there are other channels. Private tutoring. Research assistance. The Pact Library pays student assistants. I can help Flynn navigate the application process."

Everyone turned to look at Wren. Wren, who never offered anything. Wren, who had spent the whole year watching from the edges.

"Thank you," Flynn said.

Wren nodded once and returned to their journal.

Kael leaned forward, Pip settling on his shoulder with a flutter of golden feathers. "I can teach extra flying lessons. Beast Division training is always in demand. Pip needs the exercise anyway."

"Pip doesn't do labor," Tomas said.

"Pip will do what Pip is told."

Pip made a sound that was definitely disagreement.

Sera, who had been silent throughout the discussion, spoke for the first time: "The frontier settlements have their own economy. Independent of the Guilds. I can connect Flynn to the barter networks — supplies, equipment, things that don't require marks. It won't cover everything, but it helps."

"It helps a lot," Flynn said. His voice was careful, controlled — the Jester's performance, holding back the emotion that Tomas could feel surging through the bond. "All of this. I don't — I don't know how to thank you."

"You don't have to," Sera said. "That's what a cohort is."

---

The practical arrangements took three weeks to finalize.

Ivy handled the bureaucracy — the work-study applications, the scholarship petitions, the labyrinthine paperwork that PactSpire required for any deviation from standard financial arrangements. Her Forge Division training made her uniquely suited to navigating institutional systems, and she attacked the task with the same methodical precision she brought to everything else. By the end of the first week, Flynn was officially enrolled in the work-study program, assigned to evening shifts in the Pact Library under the supervision of an ancient, perpetually distracted librarian who was grateful for any assistance she could get.

Renn handled the Drakengard contribution. True to his word, Renn's family dispatched a formal letter of support within days — not a loan, not a favor to be repaid, but a statement of principle. The Drakengard line had no love for Cantrip Jesters, but they had even less love for Guilds that abused their authority. The marks came with a single condition: that Flynn continue his Combat Fundamentals training at the highest level. Renn's father, a retired Battle-Mage, had apparently taken a professional interest in the provisional combat pact.

Sera handled the barter networks. The frontier settlements were a world unto themselves, operating outside Guild jurisdiction, and Sera's family had connections that reached back three generations. Within two weeks, Flynn had access to discounted supplies, surplus equipment, and a standing invitation to spend term breaks at the Verdant Keep settlement rather than returning to Miravale. Sera's mother, a formidable Terra-Mage who managed the settlement's agricultural operations, sent a personal letter welcoming Flynn as "a friend of the frontier."

Kael handled morale. He didn't have marks to contribute — Beast Division training stipends were notoriously low — but he had energy, enthusiasm, and an absolute refusal to let anyone in the cohort feel sorry for themselves. He organized evening gatherings, dragged Flynn to every social event on campus, and made increasingly creative attempts to teach Pip to deliver motivational speeches. The speeches never worked, but the effort was appreciated.

Wren handled the quiet things. They showed up at Flynn's library shifts without being asked, helped reorganize the catalog system when Flynn fell behind, and left notes in Flynn's dormitory — small observations, encouragements, fragments of lore about Cantrip Jesters who had survived worse. Wren never asked for acknowledgment, and Flynn learned to show gratitude in ways that didn't require words.

And Tomas handled Flynn.

Not the financial arrangements — Tomas had no marks to offer, no family connections, no barter networks. But he had the bond. He had the ram. He had the steady, quiet presence that had somehow become the thing that held Flynn together when everything else was falling apart.

"You're doing it again," Flynn said, one evening after the library shift. They were in Tomas's dormitory room — a small, rough-hewn space in Hall Verdant that Tomas had never bothered to decorate — and Flynn was sitting on the edge of the bed, his Quick-Cast sparking fitfully, his expression exhausted.

"Doing what?"

"Being steady. It's very annoying."

"I've been told."

"It's also the only thing keeping me from falling apart. So I suppose I should be grateful."

"Supposed?"

Flynn looked at him. The bond between them hummed — the familiar frequency, the constant presence — and something in Flynn's expression shifted from exhausted to something softer.

"I am grateful," Flynn said. "I don't know how to say it. I've never known how to say things like this. My family doesn't do gratitude — they do obligation. If someone helps you, you owe them a favor, and the favor is documented, and the document is filed with the Guild. That's how the Wintersbloom line works."

"That's not how the herd works."

"The herd?"

"The sheep. In Veridale. When one sheep is sick, the others stand around it. They don't leave. They don't calculate favor. They just stay. My father calls it 'flock sense.' I always thought it was the same thing as what you're describing. But it's not."

"No. It's not."

"The cohort is my flock," Tomas said, and the words felt strange in his mouth — too earnest, too simple — but also true in a way that nothing else had ever been true. "You're part of it. So I stay. That's all."

Flynn was silent for a long moment. Then he laughed — the short, half-strangled laugh of someone who had just been handed something they didn't know how to hold.

"You're a shepherd," he said. "An actual, literal shepherd. You talk about people the way you talk about sheep. And somehow it's the most comforting thing anyone has ever said to me."

"Sheep are good listeners."

"Tomas."

"Yes?"

"I'm going to kiss you again."

"All right."

It was different from the solstice kiss. That had been tentative, terrified, the smallest possible question. This was an answer. Flynn's hands came up to Tomas's face — his Quick-Cast sparking, his breath warm, his whole body shaking with the particular intensity of someone who had finally stopped performing and didn't know what was left. Tomas let himself be kissed. He let himself kiss back. The ram stood by the door, watching, and its expression was definitely something.

When they separated, Flynn's eyes were bright — not with tears, with something else. Something Tomas was learning to recognize.

"Thank you," Flynn said.

"For what?"

"For being impossible to impress."

---

Spring came to PactSpire in a rush of green and gold — flowers blooming overnight, the campus warming the stones beneath everyone's feet, the Fracture Line pulsing with a brightness that the older students said was unusual for the season. The cohort trained. The cohort studied. And Flynn Wintersbloom, who had arrived at PactSpire as the pampered heir of a four-century Apothecarian line, learned to live on a work-study stipend, a Drakengard stipulation, and the quiet determination of six people who had decided he was worth fighting for.

The letter from his father stayed in his desk drawer, unburned and unanswered. Flynn wasn't ready to respond. But he was, he realized, ready to stop waiting for a response that would never come.

*You have chosen the Cantrip Jester's path. Live on it.*

He was living on it. He was living on it every day, in every class, in every training session, in every moment when the bond between him and Tomas hummed its steady, quiet frequency. And if his father ever came to PactSpire to see what his son had become, he would find — not a failure, not a disgrace, not the end of the Wintersbloom line. He would find a Cantrip Jester who had been forged in the fire of his own family's rejection.

And the fire had made him stronger.
