# Chapter Twelve: Oath-Remembered

Wren Vesper had known from the beginning that Silas Thorne was using them.

The realization had come gradually, over the course of the academic year, like frost forming on a window — imperceptible until it was too thick to see through. Thorne had recruited them in the first week of term, recognizing the Shadow-Mage hunger for recognition and offering it in carefully measured doses. Study the cohort's dynamics, Thorne had said. Observe how they interact. Report anything that might affect campus security. You have a gift for watching. Let me help you use it.

And Wren had watched. Had reported. Had fed Thorne observations about Kael's recklessness, Renn's temper, Ivy's obsessiveness, Sera's homesickness. Had justified it as a service to the campus — Thorne was a Master, a respected member of PactSpire's faculty, and if he was concerned about security, then security was worth being concerned about.

But the reports had changed over time.

In the autumn term, Thorne had asked for specific details about Tomas's aegis technique — how the ram interacted with the bond, what conditions triggered the prismatic effect. In the winter term, he had asked for details about Flynn's latent alchemical knowledge, about the Solstice Vigil, about the Headmaster's private conversations. In the spring term, he had asked Wren to retrieve a document from the Pact Library's restricted collection — a document about the masters of the Fractured North, about the conditions under which the Calamity marks could be activated from a distance.

Wren had retrieved it. Had delivered it. Had told themselves it was part of the investigation.

And now, in the first week of the summer term, Wren had found the letter.

It was in Thorne's office — the private office behind the Shadow Division wing, the one Wren had been given access to as a trusted observer. The letter was not hidden. It was on Thorne's desk, in plain sight, as if Thorne had expected Wren to find it. As if Thorne had wanted them to find it.

The letter was from the masters of the Fractured North.

It was not written in any language Wren recognized. But it was translated in Thorne's hand — a neat, precise transcription on a separate piece of parchment, the ink still fresh. Wren read it once, then again, their hands perfectly steady, their expression perfectly blank, their Shadow-Mage training keeping the panic from showing on their face.

*To the one who watches at the threshold:*

*The marked one is ready. The bond has formed. The campus is vulnerable. When the Fracture widens, the marked one will be the key. We will come through, and you will open the gate, and the pact that has held us back for five centuries will be broken at last.*

*You have done well, Watcher. Your reward awaits.*

Wren folded the translation carefully. Folded it again. Placed it back on Thorne's desk exactly where it had been, aligning the corners with the precision of someone whose hands did not shake because they could not afford to shake.

Silas Thorne was not a concerned Master. Silas Thorne was a collaborator.

And Wren had helped him.

---

The walk back to Hall Verdant was the longest of Wren's life.

The campus was in its summer configuration — warm stones, bright corridors, flowers blooming along every path. The season that was supposed to be a respite from the intensity of the academic year. But Wren felt none of the warmth. They felt the cold of the Shadow Division's underground corridors, the chill of the restricted texts, the particular, piercing horror of realizing that everything they had been told was a lie.

Thorne had told them the reports were for campus security. But the reports had been about Tomas and Flynn — specifically about the bond between them, about the conditions under which it could be strengthened or broken. Thorne had told them the document retrieval was for official research. But the document was about Calamity marks, about the way a marked individual could be used as a conduit for a larger incursion.

And Flynn was marked.

Wren stopped walking. The realization hit them with the force of a physical blow.

Flynn was marked. Tomas and Flynn were bonded. The bond was stronger than any bond in PactSpire's recent history — a provisional combat pact that had become a cultivation-partner binding, recognized by the campus and witnessed by the Headmaster. Thorne had been studying that bond. Thorne had been waiting for it to form.

And Thorne had needed Wren's reports to know when it was ready.

They had to tell someone. They had to tell the cohort. They had to tell the Headmaster. But telling anyone meant admitting what they had done — the spying, the document retrieval, the year of reports that had fed an enemy of everything PactSpire stood for. It meant losing the cohort. It meant losing the first group of people who had ever accepted Wren as one of their own.

Or they could stay silent. Could continue as Thorne's observer, pretending they had never seen the letter. Could let whatever Thorne was planning happen, and hope that someone else would stop it.

Wren took a breath. Another. The Shadow-Mage training was good for something — it taught control, taught patience, taught the ability to think clearly under pressure. They were still thinking clearly. Barely.

The cohort. They had to tell the cohort. Whatever came after, the cohort had to know.

---

The common room of Hall Verdant was empty when Wren arrived.

No — not empty. Tomas was there, the ram at his side, reading a Combat Fundamentals text by the fire. He looked up when Wren entered, and something in Wren's expression must have been visible despite all their training, because Tomas closed the book immediately.

"What happened?" he said.

"I need to talk to you. And Flynn. And the others. But you first."

"Why me first?"

"Because you're the shepherd." The word came out before Wren could stop it — they had never used the cohort's private language before, had always observed it from the outside. But the word felt right. Felt true. "You hold the flock together. I need you to hold it now."

Tomas looked at them for a long moment. The ram looked at them. Then Tomas stood, walked to the door, and called down the hallway to Flynn's room.

The cohort assembled in ten minutes.

It was the fastest assembly Wren had ever seen — Kael pulling on his training tunic, his Storm-Hawk still half-asleep on his shoulder. Sera with dirt on her hands from the cultivation chamber. Renn with his practice blade, because Renn never went anywhere without his practice blade. Ivy with her notebook, because Ivy never went anywhere without her notebook. Flynn with his Quick-Cast sparking, because the bond had told him something was wrong before Tomas had said a word.

"I have something to tell you," Wren said.

They told everything.

The recruitment in the first week. The reports, month after month. The observations about each of the cohort members — the strengths and vulnerabilities that Thorne had asked Wren to identify. The document retrieval from the restricted collection. And the letter — the translation, the Calamity words, the explicit statement that Thorne was working with the masters of the Fractured North to open the gate.

When Wren finished, the room was silent.

Kael spoke first. "You spied on us. For a year. On all of us."

"Yes."

"Everything you learned — our weaknesses, our patterns — you gave it to a Master who's working for the Calamities."

"Yes."

"How do we know you're not still working for him now?"

"Kael," Tomas said.

"It's a fair question," Wren said. "You don't know. You can't know. I've been lying to all of you for a year. There's no reason to believe I'm not lying now."

"Then why are you telling us?" Sera asked.

"Because I found the letter." Wren's voice was steady — the Shadow-Mage's control, holding the emotion behind a wall of discipline. "I found the letter, and I realized what I had been doing, and I realized that if I kept doing it, people were going to die. You. Flynn. Maybe all of us. And I couldn't —" They stopped, their voice cracking for the first time. "I couldn't let that happen. Not for recognition. Not for anything."

Flynn stepped forward. His Quick-Cast was spiking — the anxiety of a marked target, facing the revelation that his mark was not just a memory but a weapon waiting to be triggered.

"The documents I retrieved for Thorne," Wren said, turning to him. "They were about Calamity marks. About how a mark can be used to create a channel. A conduit from the Fractured North into the campus." They paused. "He knows you're marked. He's been waiting for the bond to form. I think..." The words were hard to say. "I think the bond is part of the plan. The stronger the bond, the stronger the conduit."

"The bond is a weapon," Tomas said quietly.

"I think so. Yes."

Tomas looked at Flynn. The bond between them hummed at a new frequency — not the warmth of the harvest, not the steadiness of the cultivation, but something sharper. Something like anger. Not at each other. At the person who had tried to use the most precious thing they had created as a tool of destruction.

"The Headmaster," Tomas said. "We have to tell him."

"He might already know," Ivy said. Everyone turned to look at her. "Headmaster Vowkeeper is a former combat pact partner. He's been watching the bond since the field exercise. He knows things about campus security that no one else knows. If Thorne has been working for the Fractured North, there's a chance the Headmaster has been watching him too."

"Then why hasn't he stopped him?"

"Maybe he's waiting. Maybe he needs proof. Maybe he needs us to bring it." Ivy closed her notebook. "But regardless — we have to tell him now. Tonight. Before Thorne realizes Wren has discovered the letter."

---

The Headmaster's tower was quiet at midnight.

Tomas, Flynn, and Wren stood in the center of the Grand Hall, the campus rearranging itself around them to form the staircase that led to Aldric Vowkeeper's office. The climb felt longer than it should have — each step weighted with the gravity of what they were about to reveal.

The office was as it had always been: circular, windowed, filled with the orderly ranks of Division-colored books. But the Headmaster was not behind his desk. He was standing at the window that faced the Fracture Line, his back to them, his posture speaking of a man who had not slept in a very long time.

"I know," he said, before any of them could speak. "About Thorne. About the letter. About everything."

Wren's breath caught. "How?"

"I have been watching Thorne for three years. Since the first Calamity incursion that breached the boundary zone. I suspected he was compromised, but I couldn't prove it. The campus doesn't record conversations. The Shadow Division protects its own. I needed evidence that could be presented to the Guilds, to the Masters' Council, to the pact-signatories who would have to authorize his removal." He turned, and his eyes met Wren's. "You have given me that evidence."

"I didn't mean to—"

"I know. Thorne recruited you because you were vulnerable. Because you wanted recognition. Because you were exactly the kind of student that Shadow-Mages prey upon — gifted, isolated, hungry for validation." The Headmaster's voice was not unkind. "You were manipulated. That is not the same as being complicit. You came forward. You told the truth. That is the difference between a victim and a collaborator."

"I spied on my cohort for a year."

"You did. And you will have to reckon with that. But first, we have a much larger problem." The Headmaster walked to his desk and laid a map across its surface — a map of PactSpire's boundary zone, marked with red lines that Tomas recognized as Calamity incursion paths. "The masters of the Fractured North have been probing our defenses for months. The Spawn during the field exercise was not random. It was a test — a way to measure the cohort's combat capacity and the bond's formation rate. Thorne has been feeding them data. And now, with the cultivation-partner binding complete, they believe the conduit is ready."

"The conduit," Tomas said. "Through Flynn."

"Through the bond. Flynn's mark is a channel. But a channel by itself is just an opening. The bond amplifies it. The two of you, bound together, represent a door that the Calamities can push through — if they can reach you at the right moment, in the right place."

"Can we break the bond?"

"No. Not without mutual consent, and not without destroying the cultivation partnership that the campus has already recognized. And even if you could —" The Headmaster paused. "Even if you could, it would be the wrong decision. The bond is a weapon, yes. But weapons can be aimed in either direction."

"Meaning what?"

"Meaning that if the masters of the Fractured North can use the bond as a conduit to enter the campus, then the bond can also be used as a conduit to push them back. The aegis sphere you created during the field exercise — the prismatic one — that was not just a defense. It was a counter-force. The bond absorbed the Spawn's energy and reflected it. Do that on a larger scale, with a larger target, and you have not a vulnerability but a weapon."

"Against the masters."

"Against the masters."

Tomas looked at Flynn. The bond between them hummed — the familiar frequency, the constant presence, but now layered with something new. Something like purpose.

"We have to train," Tomas said.

"Yes."

"We have to be ready before the Calamities come through."

"Yes."

"How long do we have?"

"Months. Perhaps less." The Headmaster looked at each of them in turn — Tomas, then Flynn, then Wren. "Thorne does not know we are aware of him. We will allow him to continue, to believe his plans are intact. And when the Calamities come — because they will come — we will be ready. Not because we are unafraid. Because we are prepared."

"And Wren?" Flynn asked.

"Wren will continue as Thorne's observer. But this time, the reports will be false. And every piece of information Thorne believes he is extracting from this cohort will be a weapon aimed back at him." The Headmaster looked at Wren. "Is that something you are willing to do?"

Wren was silent for a long moment. The Shadow-Mage's control held — barely. Their voice, when they spoke, was very quiet.

"Yes," they said. "After everything I've done — yes. I'll be your counter-observer. Whatever it takes."

"Then we have work to do."

---

They walked back to Hall Verdant in the deepest hour of the night, the campus dark and quiet around them. Flynn's Quick-Cast was sparking fitfully — the aftermath of fear and revelation and the particular exhaustion of someone who had just learned that their very existence was a weapon someone else was trying to wield.

"The Headmaster knew," Flynn said. "About Thorne. About the mark. About all of it."

"Apparently."

"He knew and he didn't tell us."

"He was protecting us."

"From what? The truth?"

"From acting before we were ready." Tomas stopped walking. The ram stopped beside him. "The Headmaster has been fighting the Calamities for forty years. He's made decisions we don't understand and won't understand until we've been doing this as long as he has. Maybe he was wrong not to tell us. But maybe he was right. We don't get to know yet."

Flynn looked at him. The bond hummed — the steady frequency beneath the fear.

"I'm afraid," Flynn said.

"I know."

"I'm afraid of what Thorne is planning. I'm afraid of being used as a weapon. I'm afraid of losing—" He stopped, his voice catching. "—of losing you. If the bond breaks, or if the Calamities come through, or if I can't—"

"You won't lose me."

"You can't promise that."

"I'm not promising." Tomas reached out and took Flynn's hand — the same gesture he had made at the Solstice Vigil, the same steady presence. "I'm telling you what I know. The bond is the strongest thing I've ever been part of. Stronger than the ram. Stronger than the aegis. It held against two Spawn. It will hold against whatever is coming. And I will be with you, holding it, for as long as it takes."

Flynn stared at him for a long moment. Then he laughed — the short, shaky sound of someone who had just been given something they didn't know they needed.

"You're impossible," he said.

"I've been told."

"Completely, fundamentally impossible." And then: "I love you too."

Tomas hadn't said the words. He had never said the words — not directly, not in so many syllables. But the bond carried the meaning, and Flynn had heard it, and now the words were in the air between them, irrevocable and frightening and true.

"We should sleep," Tomas said.

"We should."

"We're going to have to face Thorne tomorrow. And the Masters. And the rest of the cohort. And a training regimen that will probably kill us."

"Almost certainly."

"And at the end of it, we're going to have to fight the masters of the Fractured North."

"That does seem to be the plan."

Tomas looked at him. The ram looked at him. The campus, patient and ancient, warmed the stones beneath their feet.

"Good," Tomas said. "Then let's get started."

*End of Chapter Twelve*
