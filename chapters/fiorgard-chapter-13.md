# Chapter Thirteen: The Investigation

The investigation began with a document that did not exist.

That was how Ivy described it later, in the careful notes that would become the official record of the Thorne affair — "the investigation commenced with the identification of a gap in the institutional record." But to the cohort, gathered in the common room of Hall Verdant on a rain-soaked summer morning, it was simpler than that. They needed to know who Silas Thorne really was. And to do that, they needed to find the records that had been erased.

"The Shadow Division maintains its own archives," Ivy said, spreading her notes across the common-room table. "Separate from the Pact Library's main collection. The Division's faculty have the authority to redact or remove any record that pertains to Division affairs. Thorne has been the Division's senior Master for twelve years. It is almost certain that his personal records have been altered."

"Then how do we find what's been erased?" Kael asked.

"We don't look at the records. We look at the gaps." Ivy turned to a new page in her notebook. "Every record that exists leaves a trace — a reference in another document, a footnote in an administrative memo, a mention in someone else's file. If Thorne's personal history has been erased from the Shadow Division archives, the erasure itself leaves evidence. We triangulate the gaps, and we reconstruct what was removed."

"That sounds like a lot of work."

"It is. Which is why we're dividing the labor."

The division was strategic. Sera and Renn would interview the older faculty — Magister Brimstone, who had been at PactSpire for thirty years; Magister Willowmere, who remembered everything; Lady Shadowveil, who had been Thorne's predecessor as senior Shadow-Mage. Kael and Wren would search the main Pact Library for cross-referenced documents — administrative memos, Guild enrollment records, any scrap of information that mentioned Thorne before his appointment to the faculty. Tomas and Flynn would search the physical campus — the Shadow Division wing, the boundary archives, any place where physical records might have been stored before the electronic catalog was established.

And Ivy would coordinate — tracking every lead, cross-referencing every gap, building the case that would expose Silas Thorne for what he truly was.

---

The older faculty were, as a group, extremely reluctant to discuss Silas Thorne.

Magister Brimstone was the worst. "Thorne is a colleague," he said, when Sera and Renn cornered him in the Combat Fundamentals office. "A respected member of the faculty. I don't gossip about colleagues."

"With respect, Magister, this isn't gossip." Sera's voice was steady — the frontier diplomat's daughter, navigating institutional resistance with the patience she had learned from watching her mother negotiate settlement treaties. "We have evidence that Master Thorne is collaborating with the masters of the Fractured North. Headmaster Vowkeeper is aware of the investigation. We need to know who Thorne was before he came to PactSpire."

Brimstone's expression shifted — the particular discomfort of someone who didn't want to believe what they were hearing but also couldn't dismiss it outright. "You have evidence?"

"We do."

"What kind of evidence?"

"The kind that Headmaster Vowkeeper considers credible enough to authorize a full investigation."

Brimstone was silent for a long moment. Outside the Combat Fundamentals office, the rain continued to fall — a steady, drumming sound that filled the uncomfortable silence.

"He came to PactSpire twelve years ago," Brimstone said finally. "From the Northern Academy. That's a Shadow Division institution — the only one outside of PactSpire. It trains Shadow-Mages for Guild work, primarily. Intelligence, counter-intelligence, the kind of operations that don't make it into the public record. Thorne was recruited to replace Lady Shadowveil, who was retiring after forty years of service."

"Was there anything unusual about his appointment?"

"Unusual how?"

"Anything that raised questions. Anything that seemed out of place."

Brimstone hesitated. "Lady Shadowveil opposed the appointment. Publicly. At the Masters' Council meeting where Thorne's candidacy was confirmed, she voted against him. The only no vote. She said —" He paused, his brow furrowing with the effort of memory. "She said that he 'smelled of the Fracture.' Those were her exact words. 'Smelled of the Fracture.' The Council overruled her. Thorne's credentials were impeccable, and Lady Shadowveil was known for being... prickly. The Masters assumed it was personal."

"It wasn't personal," Renn said. "She was right."

---

Lady Shadowveil's cottage was on the northern edge of the campus, half-hidden in a grove of ancient oaks that the campus had been cultivating for centuries. It was not a place that invited visitors. The path to the door was overgrown, the windows were dark, and the only sign of habitation was a thin plume of smoke rising from the chimney.

Sera knocked. For a long moment, there was no response. Then the door opened a crack, and a voice that sounded like autumn leaves rustling in a dry wind said: "Go away."

"Lady Shadowveil. My name is Sera Verdant. I'm a first-year in the Forge Division. I need to ask you about Silas Thorne."

The door didn't close. That was something.

"Thorne," the voice said. "That name has been in my mouth for twelve years, and it has never tasted anything but bitter." The door opened wider, and Lady Shadowveil stood in the doorway — a woman so old that her age was impossible to gauge, her skin the translucent gray of deep shadow, her eyes the color of new moon. "You said you have evidence."

"We do. Thorne is collaborating with the masters of the Fractured North. We've intercepted his correspondence. Headmaster Vowkeeper is assembling a case."

Lady Shadowveil stared at them for a long moment. Then she laughed — a dry, crackling sound that was more like the breaking of old branches than actual amusement.

"Come in," she said.

---

The cottage was dark and cluttered and smelled of old parchment and woodsmoke. Lady Shadowveil led them to a sitting room where every surface was covered with books — Shadow Division texts, historical records, documents that looked like they had been pulled from archives that no longer existed.

"I told them," she said, settling into a chair that seemed to swallow her. "Twelve years ago, at the Council meeting. I told them Thorne smelled of the Fracture. I told them the Northern Academy had been compromised. I told them we were inviting a wolf into the fold." She paused. "They didn't listen. Shadow-Mages are trained to detect deception, but they forget that we can also be deceived. Thorne was good. The best I've ever seen. He had credentials. References. A record of service that was, on its surface, immaculate."

"What was wrong with it?" Renn asked.

"The gaps." Lady Shadowveil's eyes glittered in the dim light. "The same gaps you're finding now. The record of his first ten years at the Northern Academy — missing. The period between his graduation and his first faculty appointment — erased. The names of his teachers, his Guild patrons, his reference-writers — all redacted, all replaced with generic endorsements that could have been written by anyone. He had built a document that looked perfect precisely because it was hollow. And I was the only one who saw it."

"Why didn't you fight harder?"

"Because I had no proof. Suspicion is not evidence. A Shadow-Mage who accuses another Shadow-Mage without evidence is dismissed as paranoid — the occupational hazard of a profession built on paranoia. I told the Council what I could. When they overruled me, I retired. I've been waiting, for twelve years, for someone to come asking the right questions."

"We're asking now," Sera said.

"Yes. You are." Lady Shadowveil reached into a pile of documents and extracted a leather-bound journal — old, worn, the spine cracked with age. "This is my personal record of Thorne's appointment. Every conversation I had with the Northern Academy. Every discrepancy I noted in his record. Every piece of information that the Council chose to ignore. It's not proof — not by itself. But combined with the evidence you've gathered, it's the beginning of a case."

She handed the journal to Sera. Her fingers were cold — the chill of a Shadow-Mage who had spent decades living in the space between light and dark.

"There's one more thing," she said. "The masters of the Fractured North don't use intermediaries unless they have to. If Thorne is working with them directly, it means they trust him. And the only people they trust are their own." She paused. "Thorne is not from the Northern Academy. He may never have been. He was planted — a Calamity agent, inserted into the Shadow Division, groomed for twelve years to reach a position where he could open the gate."

"Then his whole career is a fabrication?"

"Not his whole career. Just his loyalties." Lady Shadowveil sat back in her chair, and her eyes closed — the exhaustion of someone who had been carrying a truth alone for too long. "Find the gaps. Fill them. And when you present your case to the Council, tell them that Evanna Shadowveil was right."

"We will," Sera said.

"You'd better. I'm running out of time to be vindicated."

---

The physical search of the campus was more productive than expected.

Tomas and Flynn spent three days in the Shadow Division wing — a network of corridors and chambers that ran beneath the main campus, perpetually dim and perpetually cold. The ram was uneasy in the darkness, its silver glow muted by the ambient Shadow-Mage energy that saturated the stone. But Tomas pressed on, and Flynn stayed beside him, and together they mapped the wing's hidden spaces.

They found the first artifact on the second day: a locked cabinet in a forgotten storage chamber, filled with documents that had been removed from the Pact Library's catalogue. Personnel records. Disciplinary reports. Correspondence between Thorne and unidentified recipients at the Northern Academy.

"These were all removed from the main collection," Flynn said, flipping through the pages. "The withdrawal stamps are Thorne's. He's been purging anything that mentions him."

"Then why keep them?"

"Because he's arrogant. He doesn't think anyone will find them. And he wants the record — somewhere, for himself. Even traitors want to be remembered."

They found the second artifact on the third day: a hidden alcove behind a false wall in Thorne's private study. Inside the alcove: a Calamity-stone — the same kind that had formed inside the Spawn during the field exercise, black and pulsing with the particular wrongness that Tomas now associated with the Fractured North. Beside the stone: a map of the campus boundary zone, marked with incursion paths and dates. And beside the map: a list of names.

Flynn read the names aloud. "Aelric Stonewall. Mira Halestorm. Cade Brightwood. Elara Frostmere." He stopped, his Quick-Cast sparking violently. "These are all PactSpire graduates. Combat Division, all of them. Graduated in the last ten years."

"Thorne was their Master."

"Yes. And according to these notes —" Flynn's voice was very steady, the Jester's control holding the horror at bay. "According to these notes, all four of them are dead. Killed in 'training accidents' and 'boundary patrol incidents' within two years of graduation."

"Thorne killed them."

"The notes don't say that explicitly. But they say the deaths were 'necessary' and that the 'control group' had been 'validated.'" Flynn set the document down. "He used them. These were his students, and he used them as test subjects for whatever he's planning to do with the bond."

Tomas looked at the list of names. Four dead graduates. Four students who had trusted their Master and been killed for it. And now the same Master was watching his cohort — watching him and Flynn — waiting to do the same thing.

"We have to take this to the Headmaster," Tomas said.

"We have to take everything."

---

The full investigation took three weeks.

By the end of the third week, Ivy had compiled a document that ran to forty-seven pages — a comprehensive case against Silas Thorne, backed by Lady Shadowveil's journal, the recovered artifacts from the Shadow Division wing, Wren's testimony about the letter, and the testimony of every faculty member the cohort had interviewed.

The case was damning. Thorne had falsified his credentials. He had been working with the masters of the Fractured North for at least twelve years — possibly longer. He had manipulated institutional records to erase evidence of his activities. He had used his position as a Shadow-Mage Master to identify and eliminate students who might have discovered his plans. And he had been preparing, for years, to use the bond between Tomas and Flynn as a conduit for a Calamity incursion.

Headmaster Vowkeeper read the document in silence. When he finished — forty-seven pages, read slowly and carefully, absorbing every detail — he set it down on his desk and looked at the seven students who had built it.

"This is extraordinary work," he said. "It is also extremely dangerous. Thorne will know something is happening. He will have noticed the interviews with the older faculty, the searches of the Shadow Division wing, the patterns of behavior that don't match his expectations. He is already suspicious."

"What do we do?" Tomas asked.

"We act before he does. The Masters' Council meets in three days. I will present the case then. Thorne will be detained. The campus will be placed on heightened alert. And we will begin preparing for the Calamity incursion that — based on the intercepted correspondence — is now imminent."

"Imminent how soon?"

"Weeks. Perhaps days. The last communication we intercepted suggested that the bond is ready and the conduit is open. They're waiting for the right moment to strike. When they strike, Thorne will be their inside agent." The Headmaster paused. "Unless we strike first."

"How?"

"By turning the trap around. By using the bond — not as a conduit for them to enter, but as a weapon to push them back. And by facing the masters of the Fractured North on our terms, not theirs."

The words hung in the air. Seven students — first-years, barely a year into their studies — facing the same darkness that had broken the world five centuries ago.

"We'll be ready," Tomas said.

The ram, standing at his side, made a sound that was definitely agreement.
