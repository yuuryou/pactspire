# Chapter Two: The First Clash

The morning bell sang at a frequency designed to bypass the ear and strike directly at the spine. Tomas had never heard anything like it. In Veridale, morning was announced by sheep and sunlight and the sound of his father stoking the hearth — all of which arrived at subtly different times depending on the season. PactSpire's bell arrived at exactly seven o'clock, every morning, regardless of weather, mood, or the desperate desire of sixteen-year-olds to remain unconscious for another hour.

Tomas opened his eyes. The ram was still in the corner, head lowered, watching the door.

"Did you sleep?" Tomas asked.

The ram did not answer. Tomas was not sure if totems needed sleep. The ram had not moved from its position since Tomas closed his eyes. It might have been standing guard all night. It might have been dormant. Tomas had no way to tell, and that bothered him more than he wanted to admit.

He dressed in the clothes his father had packed — two changes of wool shirt, one pair of trousers that had belonged to his older brother before the fever took him, the sheepskin vest his mother had made. The other students he'd glimpsed in the hall last night had worn linen and cotton and something that shimmered slightly in the lamplight. Tomas had never seen fabric shimmer before. He had not known fabric could.

The ram walked beside him as he left the room. It always walked beside him now. He was beginning to understand that this was not a choice the ram made but a fact of its existence — it went where he went, stood where he stood, watched what he watched. The ram was him, or a part of him, or a version of him that had been carved out and given form. The distinction didn't seem to matter.

Hall Verdant's common room was already occupied. Three students sat at the long wooden table, none of whom Tomas recognized. They looked at him — at the ram — and something in their expressions shifted. Not hostility. Calculation. The look of people deciding whether someone was worth knowing.

Tomas nodded once and kept walking. He had spent sixteen years learning to read the body language of sheep. People were not that different.

***

The first class of the morning was Combat Fundamentals, held in the Training Ring's eastern arena. The arena was a broad oval of packed earth, open to the sky, with tiered stone benches on three sides and a raised observation platform on the fourth. Practice weapons lined the walls — wooden swords, padded staves, blunted spears. The air smelled of sweat and crushed grass and something faintly metallic that Tomas recognized as old blood, scrubbed clean but never quite gone.

Twenty students stood in loose formation. Tomas counted seven Sword Division — recognizable by the way they held themselves, the instinctive readiness in their shoulders — and the rest a mix of Seed, Frontier, and one nervous-looking Forge apprentice who kept adjusting his spectacles.

Magister Lyra Brimstone stood at the center of the arena with her arms folded. She was short for a Drakengard native — barely shoulder-height to Tomas — but built with the compact density of something forged under pressure. Her hair was iron-grey and cropped close to her skull. The scar that bisected her left eyebrow gave her expression a permanent quality of skepticism.

"Your Class tells you what you are," she said, without preamble. Her voice carried without being raised — the kind of voice that had spent decades being heard over the clash of steel. "It tells you nothing about what you can do. The gap between a Totemic Sentinel and a farmhand who happens to have a ram is training. The gap between an apprentice and a corpse is the same thing. We begin now."

She walked the line of students, looking at each of them. When she reached Tomas, she stopped.

"Totemic Sentinel."

"Yes, Magister."

"Your totem is fully manifested. That's unusual for a first-year. Most Sentinels take weeks to stabilize the form."

Tomas didn't know what to say to that. The ram had been there from the moment he woke. It had never been anything but solid.

"It was like that from the start," he said.

Magister Brimstone's eyes flicked to the ram, then back to Tomas. "Then you've been guarding something since you were old enough to walk. The Class doesn't give you what you want. It gives you what you are." She turned away before Tomas could respond. "Pair off. Totemic Sentinel, you're with the Pyromancer."

The Pyromancer stepped forward from the Sword Division cluster, and Tomas understood immediately that this was going to be a problem.

Renn Ashvale was tall — nearly Tomas's height — with the copper-brown skin of the Drakengard plains and the kind of physical confidence that came from never having lost. He moved like someone who had been trained to fight since before he could read, which Tomas suspected was exactly the case. A faint heat shimmer hung around his hands, the unconscious mana-leakage of a Pyromancer who hadn't yet learned to contain it.

"Herdsson," Renn said, looking at the ram. "Veridale. That's sheep country."

"It is."

"And your totem is a sheep."

"It's a ram."

Renn's smile was not friendly. "I know what a sheep looks like."

Magister Brimstone had moved on to the next pair. She was not watching. Tomas understood that this was deliberate. She wanted to see what happened.

"Basic drill," Renn said, settling into a stance. "I attack. You defend. The totem counts. Let's see if a sheep farmer can take a hit."

The first firebolt came without warning.

It was not a combat spell — Renn was not stupid enough to throw lethal magic in a training arena — but it was also not gentle. A fist-sized sphere of compressed heat, fast enough to blur, aimed at Tomas's chest. The ram moved before Tomas could think. It was simply there, between him and the firebolt, absorbing the heat into its spectral form. Tomas felt the impact as a pressure behind his sternum — not pain, exactly, but the memory of pain, the shape of a bruise that never formed.

Renn's eyes widened. "It moved on its own."

"It does that."

"Again."

This time Renn threw two bolts in quick succession, one high and one low. The ram intercepted the first, but the second slipped past and struck Tomas in the shoulder. Real pain this time — a sharp, immediate heat that made his arm go numb. He staggered.

"Your totem has a blind spot," Renn said. "You."

Tomas shook out his arm. The numbness was already fading, mana flooding the injury with the accelerated healing that was part of the Sentinel Class. "I know."

"Then fix it."

"I'm trying."

"Try faster."

The third exchange was not a drill. Renn came at him with a sequence of six bolts, each one faster than the last, driving Tomas backward across the arena floor. The ram intercepted three. Tomas dodged one. Two hit — his thigh, his ribs — and the pain was real enough now that spots danced at the edge of his vision.

But he was still standing.

Renn stopped. The heat shimmer around his hands faded slightly. He was breathing harder than the drill should have required — Tomas realized that rapid-fire casting was mana-intensive, even for small bolts — and something in his expression had changed. Not respect. Not yet. But the absence of dismissal.

"You took five hits," Renn said. "Most first-years drop after two."

"I've been hit before."

"By what? Angry sheep?"

"By weather," Tomas said. "By hunger. By years that didn't cooperate." He straightened, and the ram moved back to his side. "I don't know much about fighting, Ashvale. But I know how to still be standing when the other thing is tired."

Renn looked at him for a long moment. Then he laughed — a short, surprised sound that seemed to surprise him as much as Tomas.

"Fine. We'll do this properly." He raised his hands again, and this time there was a structure to the heat — a deliberate pattern, something more than instinct. "Watch the ram. Learn its rhythm. You're not commanding it. You're dancing with it. Start acting like you know the steps."

The fourth exchange was slower. Deliberate. Renn's bolts came at measured intervals, giving Tomas time to feel the ram's movements — not as separate actions but as extensions of his own intent. By the seventh bolt, he was no longer thinking about it. He was simply standing, and the ram was simply there, and the bolts were simply not hitting him.

"Better," Renn said. "Now try it while moving."

---

Across the arena, Flynn Wintersbloom was having a much worse morning.

He had been assigned to Magister Lyra's Stage fundamentals session — a smaller group of eight students, all Stage Division, all looking at him with the particular curiosity reserved for nobles who ended up in the wrong Class. Flynn had spent his life being looked at. He was used to it. What he was not used to was being looked at by people who were better than him at what he was supposed to be doing.

"Quick-Cast is the Cantrip Jester's foundation," Magister Brimstone's assistant — a lean Journeyman named Pell whose Class Flynn hadn't caught — was saying. "You don't prepare. You react. The spell forms between impulse and action. If you have time to think about it, you've already failed."

Flynn had time to think about it.

He stood in the practice circle, facing a training construct — a head-sized sphere of woven reeds suspended from a wooden arm, enchanted to dart unpredictably around the circle. The exercise was simple: hit it with a spark. Any spark. The size didn't matter. The precision didn't matter. All that mattered was speed.

The construct dodged. Flynn's spark went wide.

"Again," Pell said.

The construct dodged. Flynn's spark went wide.

"Again."

Wide.

The other students had stopped practicing to watch. Flynn could feel their attention like a physical weight — which was ironic, because Cantrip Jesters were supposed to draw power from audiences, and right now all he felt was the slow drain of public failure.

"You're thinking," Pell said.

"I'm aware."

"Stop."

"That's not how thinking works."

"It is for a Jester." Pell stepped closer, lowering his voice. "Look. You're a Wintersbloom. You've been trained to prepare. Preparation is the enemy of improvisation. Your Class doesn't want the version of you that plans. It wants the version of you that acts."

"That version doesn't exist," Flynn said.

"Then make one."

Flynn closed his eyes. He thought about the awakening — the kaleidoscope, the fracturing patterns, the sense of motion that had felt almost like flight. He thought about his father's closed door, his mother's averted eyes, the seventeen patents that would never carry his name. He thought about Tomas Herdsson, standing in the Grand Hall, telling the truth with dirt on his boots and a ram at his side.

When he opened his eyes, he didn't aim. He didn't plan. He just moved.

The spark left his hand before he knew he'd cast it. The construct dodged — but the spark curved, tracking, following the motion with an intelligence that wasn't Flynn's because Flynn hadn't had time to give it intelligence. It struck the construct dead center, and the reed sphere burst into brief, harmless flame.

Silence.

"Again," Pell said, and this time his voice was different.

The construct reset. Flynn didn't close his eyes this time. He didn't think about his father or Tomas or the seventeen patents. He simply let the spark happen, and it happened, and it hit, and it hit again, and by the fifth repetition he was laughing — a sound he barely recognized as his own.

"Good," Pell said. "Now you're a Jester."

Flynn looked at his hands. They were still uncalloused, still Wintersbloom-pale, still the hands that would never hold a stabilization brew. But they had just done something his father's hands could not do.

He wasn't sure yet whether that was victory or treason.

---

The second class of the morning was Pact Theory, held in a lecture hall in the Classroom Ring that could seat two hundred students and was, at this hour, about half full.

Magister Silas Thorne stood at the lectern.

He was a tall man, thin in the way of someone who had been hungry once and never forgotten it. His hair was black, slicked back from a high forehead, and his eyes were the pale grey of a winter sky that hadn't decided whether to snow. He wore the grey robes of the Pact Law faculty, but there was something about the way he moved — a stillness, a patience — that made Tomas think of a predator waiting for something to come within reach.

"Pact Theory," Silas said, surveying the room. His voice was soft, almost conversational. "The study of how promises become power. How words, spoken with intent, reshape the mana-current of the world. How oaths bind not only the swearer but reality itself."

He paused, letting the silence stretch. The room was very still.

"The Oath-Breaking," Silas continued, "is always taught as tragedy. The emperor's betrayal. The shattering of the primordial pact. It is framed — always — as the moment when everything went wrong. I would like to propose a different reading."

Tomas saw several students shift uncomfortably. A Seed Division girl in the second row exchanged glances with her neighbor.

"What if the Oath-Breaking was not failure but discovery? What if the emperor, in his wisdom, understood something we have spent five centuries refusing to acknowledge — that pacts are not sacred bonds but negotiated constraints? That an oath, at its core, is a cage?"

A Sword Division boy in the front row raised his hand. Silas nodded.

"Magister, the Oath-Restored Covenant teaches that pacts are the foundation of civilization. Without them, mana is chaos."

"The Oath-Restored Covenant," Silas said, with a smile that did not reach his eyes, "was written by the Guilds. The Guilds whose power depends entirely on maintaining the pact system. I am not asking you to discard your beliefs. I am asking you to think about where those beliefs came from. Every system justifies itself. The question is whether the justification is true."

Tomas felt the ram tense beside him. He didn't know why. The lecture was unsettling, but it was just words. Wasn't it?

In the back row, half-hidden in shadow despite the morning light streaming through the tall windows, Wren Vesper was taking notes. They were, Tomas noticed, far more detailed than anyone else's. Wren's pen moved with the steady precision of someone who was not just recording but cataloguing — sorting information into categories Tomas couldn't guess at.

The Vespermarch student had said nothing since the term began. In the dormitory hall, in the dining hall, in the corridors between classes, Wren was a presence that registered more by absence — the space where a person should be, occupied by silence. Tomas had tried to catch their eye once. Wren had looked through him as if he were made of glass.

Silas's lecture continued, moving through the seven types of voluntary pact, the three categories of violation, the disputed territory of implied consent. It was, Tomas had to admit, brilliant. The man knew his subject. But there was something beneath the knowledge — a current of anger, or perhaps something older than anger, running under the words like underground water.

When the bell sang the end of the period, Tomas gathered his notes — sparse, barely legible, nothing like Wren's meticulous pages — and headed for the door. Silas was speaking quietly to a small cluster of students who had stayed behind to ask questions. Wren was among them, and as Tomas passed, he heard Silas say, "Your perspective on Vespermarch pact philosophy is unusual. I'd like to discuss it further. My office hours are posted."

Wren nodded once. Tomas kept walking. It was none of his business.

The ram looked back over its shoulder and made a sound that was not quite a bleat.

***

By the end of the first week, Tomas had learned several things.

He had learned that his room in Hall Verdant grew warmer when he was upset and cooler when he was calm — the living campus responding to something he couldn't feel but could apparently emit. He had learned that his ram totem could not pass through walls, despite being technically spectral, but could pass through people without them noticing. He had learned that the dining hall's kitchen was staffed by a Forge Division Artisan who had figured out how to cook with Thermal Snap and was very, very proud of it.

He had also learned that Flynn Wintersbloom was in three of his five classes, despite being in a completely different Division.

"It's the dual-apprentice rotation," the registrar said, when Tomas went to ask about it. She was the same grey-haired woman from registration day, and she looked no happier to see him. "Sword and Stage have overlapping fundamentals this year. Pact Theory, Combat Fundamentals, Mana Cultivation. The campus schedules by the cohort, not the individual."

"The campus schedules?"

"Did you think we did it by hand?"

Tomas looked at his schedule again. Combat Fundamentals with Magister Brimstone. Pact Theory with Magister Thorne. Mana Cultivation with a Seed Division Master he hadn't met yet. And — this was the part that made his stomach tighten — Apprentice Duty Rotation, which assigned first-year students to work assignments across the campus. Groundskeeping. Kitchen service. Library organization.

Flynn Wintersbloom was in his rotation group.

All seven of them were in his rotation group.

The campus, Tomas was beginning to understand, did not believe in coincidence.

---

The first rotation assignment was the Pact Library. Evening shift, two hours after dinner, when the shelves were supposed to be reorganized and the reading tables wiped clean and the lamps refilled with the slow-burning oil that smelled faintly of sage.

Tomas arrived first, because Tomas arrived first to everything — a habit born of a childhood where being late meant a sheep died. The Library was a vast circular chamber occupying the entire ground floor of the Spire's eastern wing. The ceiling was lost in shadow, though Tomas could see the faint glimmer of what might have been constellations painted across its surface. The shelves rose three stories high, connected by spiral staircases that occasionally rearranged themselves. Books drifted between shelves when no one was looking, migrating toward students who needed them.

"First rule," said the librarian — a wiry woman named Magister Vellum whose Class Tomas couldn't determine and was afraid to ask about — "is that the books choose you. Don't fight it. If a book doesn't want to be shelved, it won't be shelved. If a book follows you home, bring it back. They get lonely."

"Books get lonely?"

"Everything gets lonely." She handed him a cart loaded with unshelved volumes. "Start with the east wall. The History section has been migrating into Mythology again. They're always trying to merge. Someone needs to explain to them that the categories exist for a reason."

Tomas pushed the cart toward the east wall, the ram walking beside him. The other six students arrived in a loose cluster — Renn, already complaining about manual labor; Sera Thornwell, silent and watchful as always; Ivy Belladonna, who had been placed in the Seed and Forge dual-apprentice track and was already carrying a notebook full of observations about the Library's botanical specimens; Kael Highwind, whose juvenile Storm-Hawk was circling the upper shelves with an interest that worried Tomas; Wren Vesper, who immediately vanished into the shadowiest corner of the room; and Flynn, who looked at Tomas with an expression that Tomas couldn't read.

"You're early," Flynn said.

"I'm always early."

"Of course you are."

They worked in silence for a while. Tomas shelved books. Flynn wiped tables. The ram watched both of them.

"I saw you in Combat Fundamentals," Flynn said eventually. "Against the Pyromancer."

"I saw you in Stage fundamentals. Against the reed ball."

Flynn's jaw tightened. "That was different."

"No, it wasn't."

"The reed ball wasn't trying to hurt me."

"Neither was Renn. Not really." Tomas slid a book into place — a worn volume on the history of the Miravale court — and the shelf hummed approvingly. "He was trying to find out if I was worth his time. You were doing the same thing with the construct. Finding out if you were worth your own time."

Flynn was silent for a moment. Then, quietly: "And what did you find out?"

"That I can take a hit," Tomas said. "That I need to learn to dance with the ram instead of letting it do all the work. That Renn Ashvale is better than me at everything that matters, but that's fine, because I wasn't planning to be better than him. I was planning to be harder to kill."

Flynn made a sound that might have been a laugh. "You're strange, Herdsson."

"I've been told."

"I was being insulting."

"I know."

The Library settled around them. Books rustled on their shelves, rearranging themselves with soft papery sighs. The Storm-Hawk had found a perch on the highest shelf and was watching the lamps with predatory interest. Kael was trying to coax it down with a strip of dried meat. Sera was reading the book she was supposed to be shelving, her expression unreadable.

"We're supposed to be enemies," Flynn said. It was almost a question.

"Are we?"

"Your Division against mine. Your kingdom against mine. Your Class against my Class."

"My kingdom doesn't have anything against yours," Tomas said. "We don't have enough spare energy for grudges. The sheep keep us busy."

"Your Class, then. Sword against Stage."

Tomas considered this. He looked at the ram — solid, patient, the wall made manifest. Then at Flynn — sharp-edged, brilliant, a kaleidoscope in a family of measuring cups.

"I don't think the campus put us in the same rotation by accident," Tomas said. "And I don't think it cares about grudges. I think it cares about what grows."

Flynn looked at him for a long moment. His Cantrip Jester senses — the ones he was only beginning to understand — registered something he couldn't name. A steadiness. A center of gravity. Tomas Herdsson was not impressive, in the way Flynn had been taught to measure impressiveness. He was not clever or wealthy or well-connected. But he was *there*, completely there, in a way that made Flynn feel, for the first time in a week, like he might be allowed to stop performing.

"I don't know how to do that," Flynn said.

"Do what?"

"What you did. In the Grand Hall. Tell the truth." Flynn's voice was barely above a whisper. "I've been performing for sixteen years. I don't know if there's anything real under it."

Tomas slid the last book into place. The shelf hummed. The ram flicked one ear.

"Then stop performing," Tomas said. "Right now. Just stop. See what's left."

Flynn opened his mouth to say something cutting, something defensive, something that would put a wall between himself and the farm boy who was looking at him with those too-steady eyes. But the words wouldn't come. For the first time in his life, Flynn Wintersbloom had nothing to say.

The silence was not uncomfortable. It was, Flynn realized, the first real silence he had ever shared with another person — the first silence he had not felt compelled to fill.

From the shadowed corner of the Library, Wren Vesper watched them both with eyes that reflected no light at all.

---

That night, Tomas dreamed of fire.

It was not his dream. He knew this the way he knew his own name — with a certainty that bypassed rational thought. The flames were not the orange and yellow of ordinary combustion but a shifting prism of colors, a kaleidoscope of heat, and at the heart of the fire was a thin boy with too-sharp features, standing alone, burning but not consumed.

Tomas woke with a gasp. The ram was at his side — closer than usual, its spectral bulk pressing against the edge of his bed.

"I'm fine," Tomas said. "It wasn't my dream."

The ram did not believe him. Tomas wasn't sure he believed himself. He lay back, staring at the ceiling, and tried to remember the name of the feeling that had flooded through him as he watched the boy in the fire.

It was not pity. It was not concern. It was something older, something that felt like standing in front of sheep in a storm, knowing that if he moved, things behind him would die.

*If it protects you, you protect it back.*

He didn't know what the dream meant. He didn't know why the campus had put a Cantrip Jester in his rotation, or why the ram watched Flynn with something that was not quite recognition. But he knew, with the same certainty that had told him the dream was not his, that this was not the last time Flynn Wintersbloom would need someone to stand in front of him.

The walls of Hall Verdant hummed a frequency that felt almost like approval.

In Hall Clarion, three rings away, Flynn lay awake in his too-large bed, staring at his too-good view, feeling the echo of a dream that wasn't his — a dream of green hills, grey skies, and a ram that stood between the dreamer and everything that wanted to hurt him.

He didn't know what it meant. But he knew that when he woke, for the first time since his awakening, he had not felt alone.

The campus breathed around them both, patient and ancient, waiting.

---

The second week brought the first formal challenge.

It happened in the Training Ring, during the afternoon combat session. Renn Ashvale had been sparring with a Forge Division apprentice — a stocky girl from Ashfall who fought with a rune-etched quarterstaff and was good enough to make Renn work for his victories — when he suddenly broke off and turned to face the observation benches.

"Herdsson," he called. "Formal challenge. Totemic Sentinel against Pyromancer. Full-spectrum defensive drill."

The arena went quiet. Formal challenges were not forbidden, but they were not encouraged either. They were the old way — the Drakengard way — of establishing hierarchy. The first-year students on the benches exchanged glances. Some looked excited. Some looked worried. Most looked at Tomas with the particular curiosity of people waiting to see if someone was going to get hurt.

Tomas stood. The ram rose beside him. Magister Brimstone, who had been observing from the raised platform, made no move to intervene.

"Terms?" Tomas asked.

"Standard defensive drill," Renn said. "You hold position in the center circle. I attack from range. You can use the ram, defensive auras, whatever you have. You step out of the circle, you lose. You go down, you lose. You last three minutes without breaking, you win."

Three minutes. Against a Pyromancer who had been training since childhood.

"Accepted," Tomas said.

He walked to the center circle — a ring of white stone fifteen feet across — and settled into a stance. The ram stood at his left shoulder, head high, its spectral form flickering slightly as it attuned to his intent. Tomas had spent the past week learning the rhythm Renn had shown him. The dance between instinct and action. The space where the ram moved before he thought.

It wasn't enough. He knew it wasn't enough. Renn Ashvale was better than him. But Tomas had not come to PactSpire to be better than anyone. He had come to learn.

"Begin," Magister Brimstone said.

The first minute was manageable. Renn opened with a measured sequence — fast enough to test, slow enough to learn from. Tomas let the ram intercept the bolts aimed at his chest and head while he dodged the ones aimed at his legs. The pattern was familiar from their drills. He could read Renn's tells now — the slight shift of weight before a high bolt, the flicker of heat around the left hand before a spread pattern.

The second minute was harder. Renn increased the pace. Two bolts became three. Three became five. The ram was everywhere at once — Tomas could feel the strain in his own chest as the totem absorbed hit after hit, the pressure building behind his sternum like a held breath. His dodging grew sloppier. A bolt clipped his shoulder. Another struck his hip. The pain was real but distant, filtered through the ram's absorption into something manageable.

The third minute was a wall.

Renn stopped holding back. The bolts came in a continuous stream — not aimed at individual points but saturating the entire circle, leaving nowhere to dodge. Tomas dropped to one knee, and the ram expanded — a pulse of spectral energy that formed a half-dome shield, catching the bolts in a net of silver light. The pressure in Tomas's chest became pain. Real pain. The kind of pain that meant his mana was running out.

*Thirty seconds*, he thought. The ram was flickering now, its form becoming translucent at the edges. Another ten bolts struck the shield. Another ten. The pain was a roar in his ears, drowning out the sound of the audience, the arena, the world. Tomas couldn't see. He couldn't think. He could only kneel and hold and refuse to fall.

"Time," Magister Brimstone said.

The bolts stopped. The shield collapsed. Tomas was still kneeling, still in the circle, still breathing. The ram was barely visible — a fading outline, more afterimage than presence — but it was there. Standing. Like Tomas.

Renn walked to the edge of the circle and looked down at him. His expression was unreadable.

"You didn't break," he said.

"I don't," Tomas managed.

"No." Renn extended a hand. "You don't."

Tomas took it. Renn pulled him to his feet with surprising gentleness, and for a moment they stood face to face — the Pyromancer and the Sentinel, the warrior and the shepherd. Something passed between them that wasn't friendship but was adjacent to it. The recognition of equal commitment to different things.

"You need to learn to direct the shield," Renn said. "Not just hold it. The half-dome is good, but it burns mana too fast. If you shape it — angle it — you can deflect instead of absorb. Same protection, quarter of the cost."

"I'll learn."

"Good." Renn released his hand. "Because I'm going to challenge you again next week. And I won't hold back."

"I wouldn't expect you to."

Renn nodded once and walked away. The crowd on the benches dispersed, buzzing with the particular energy of students who had just watched something interesting. Magister Brimstone caught Tomas's eye from the observation platform and gave him a single nod — the closest thing to praise she was likely to offer.

Tomas stood in the center circle, breathing, feeling the ram slowly re-solidify at his side. Across the arena, near the equipment racks, Flynn Wintersbloom was watching him. Their eyes met. Flynn's expression was complicated — admiration and resentment and something else, something Tomas couldn't read.

Flynn looked away first.

Tomas didn't understand why that felt like losing.

*End of Chapter Two*
