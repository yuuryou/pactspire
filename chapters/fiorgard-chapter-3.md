# Chapter Three: The Challenge

The formal duel was scheduled for the third Friday of the term, because Renn Ashvale believed in doing things properly.

In Drakengard, a formal challenge required three things: a public venue, an impartial adjudicator, and stakes that mattered. Renn had secured the Training Ring's central arena for the purpose — the same arena where Combat Fundamentals had become the unofficial proving ground of the first-year cohort. He had requested Magister Brimstone as adjudicator, which was either brave or foolish, depending on how one felt about being judged by a woman who had killed seventeen Calamity-spawn and regarded most student disputes as beneath her attention. And for stakes, he had proposed the oldest currency in any academy: reputation.

"The loser," Renn announced to the gathered crowd of approximately sixty students who had somehow learned about the duel despite no formal announcement being made, "acknowledges the winner's primacy in Combat Fundamentals for the remainder of the term. The loser yields the first sparring slot. The loser addresses the winner as *senior combatant* until Solstice."

A ripple of reaction went through the crowd. The terms were not cruel, but they were pointed. Renn was not asking for submission. He was asking for acknowledgment of hierarchy — the Drakengard way, where status was earned through demonstration rather than inherited through blood.

Tomas stood in the challenger's circle — a smaller ring adjacent to the main arena, where the challenged party waited while the challenger addressed the crowd — and tried to decide if he was nervous.

He was. But the ram was not, and somehow that helped.

"Terms accepted," he said, when Renn finished. "On one condition."

Renn raised an eyebrow. "Name it."

"If I win, you stop calling my ram a sheep."

A beat of silence. Then laughter — not mocking, but surprised, the sound of an audience that had expected deference and gotten something else. Renn's expression flickered through several emotions in rapid succession before settling on something that looked almost like respect.

"Agreed," he said.

Magister Brimstone descended from the observation platform with the measured pace of someone who had seen a hundred duels and expected to see a hundred more before she retired. She carried a short staff of grey wood — pact-wood, Tomas recognized, the kind that had been grown rather than carved — and when she struck it against the arena floor, the stone rang like a bell.

"First-year formal challenge. Totemic Sentinel versus Pyromancer. Standard defensive-format rules, three-minute rounds, three rounds total. Victory by ring-out, incapacitation, forfeit, or adjudicator decision. The adjudicator's decision is final. Strikes to the head, throat, and spine are restricted. Lethal intent will result in immediate expulsion and referral to the Kingdom Justices. Understood?"

"Understood," Tomas said.

"Understood," Renn said.

"Then take your positions."

The duel circle was larger than the training circle — thirty feet across, ringed with a low stone wall that marked the boundary. Tomas took his position at the center. The ram stood at his left shoulder, head up, its spectral form sharper than it had been two weeks ago. Tomas had been practicing. The ram's edges no longer flickered. Its horns had developed a faint silvery sheen, like moonlight on water.

Renn took his position at the southern edge of the circle. His hands were already blazing — not the casual heat-shimmer of the training drills but a controlled, intense flame that cast long shadows across the arena floor. He had been practicing too.

"Round one," Magister Brimstone said. "Begin."

---

The first round was Tomas's.

He had learned, in the two weeks since their first training session, that the ram could do more than intercept. It could project. The aegis — the protective aura that was the Sentinel's primary defensive tool — could be shaped into a wedge, a dome, a wall. Tomas had spent every evening in the practice rooms, testing angles and configurations, learning the way mana flowed through the bond between himself and his totem. The ram knew things he didn't. The trick was learning to listen.

Renn opened with his signature saturation pattern — a wide spread of firebolts designed to overwhelm the Sentinel's intercept capacity. But Tomas had seen this pattern before. He angled the aegis into a sloping wedge, and the bolts that struck it skidded sideways rather than absorbing straight on. The mana cost was a fraction of what the half-dome had required. Tomas felt the impact as a series of glancing blows — bruises that never quite landed — and held his ground.

"Good," Renn called, and shifted tactics.

The second exchange was heat-pressure. Not firebolts. A sustained wave of thermal energy that pressed against the aegis like a tide, testing its integrity, looking for weak points. Tomas could feel the ram straining — not breaking, but working. The temperature inside the circle rose by ten degrees, then twenty. Sweat beaded on Tomas's forehead. His breathing grew ragged.

But he didn't move.

"You're standing in an oven," Renn said. "How long can you hold?"

"Longer than you can burn," Tomas said, and pushed back.

The aegis flared — a pulse of silver light that shoved the heat-wave back three feet. Renn's eyes widened. He hadn't known Tomas could do that. Neither had Tomas, exactly. The ram had simply done it, and Tomas had let it, and the result was a gap in Renn's offense that Tomas exploited by advancing two steps and resetting his stance.

Renn was breathing harder now. Sustained thermal pressure was mana-intensive, and Tomas's unexpected counter had cost him momentum. He switched back to targeted bolts — three, five, seven — but Tomas was reading the rhythm now, angling the aegis to deflect rather than absorb, and the bolts that did get through were glancing hits that his accelerated healing absorbed before they could slow him down.

"Round one to the Sentinel," Magister Brimstone called.

The crowd murmured. This was not the expected outcome. Renn Ashvale was supposed to win the first round, dominate the second, and close in the third. That was how these duels went. The Pyromancer always won the early exchanges. The Sentinel's job was to survive long enough for the Pyromancer to exhaust themselves.

Tomas had just done something different.

Renn walked to the edge of the circle and drank from a waterskin. His expression was no longer confident. It was calculating.

"You've been practicing," he said.

"You told me to."

"I didn't expect you to listen."

"I always listen," Tomas said. "I just don't always talk about it."

---

The second round was Renn's.

He opened with a technique Tomas had never seen — a spiraling lance of compressed flame that punched through the angled aegis like a hot knife through butter. The ram absorbed the hit, but the impact staggered Tomas backward, and for the first time in the duel, his feet shifted toward the boundary line.

Renn didn't give him time to recover. A second lance, a third, each one precisely aimed at the junction points where Tomas's aegis-angle couldn't quite cover. Tomas realized what was happening — Renn had spent the first round mapping his defensive geometry, identifying the angles where deflection became impossible, and was now targeting those angles with surgical precision.

It was brilliant. It was also, Tomas understood with a sinking feeling, exactly what a trained duelist did against an opponent who relied on instinct.

The fourth lance struck Tomas in the chest — not absorbed by the ram, not deflected by the aegis, but a direct hit that knocked the wind out of him and sent him stumbling. The fifth hit his shoulder. The sixth, his thigh. Tomas went down on one knee, the ram flickering beside him, and the boundary line was three feet behind him and closing fast.

"Yield," Renn said.

"No."

"That wasn't a question."

Tomas planted his hands on the arena floor and pushed himself up. His chest was a constellation of bruises. His left leg wouldn't take his full weight. The ram was translucent at the edges again, mana-depleted, barely holding form. But it was still there. And Tomas was still in the circle.

"Round two to the Pyromancer," Magister Brimstone called.

Renn lowered his hands. The flames dimmed. He was watching Tomas with an expression that was no longer calculating. It was confused.

"Why aren't you yielding?"

"Because the round isn't over until the adjudicator calls it," Tomas said. "And because I'm still standing."

"You're barely standing."

"Barely is still standing."

Renn opened his mouth, closed it, and shook his head. "You're the most frustrating person I've ever fought."

"I've been told."

---

The third round was the one that mattered.

Both of them were tired. Renn's mana reserves were visibly depleted — his flames were smaller now, more tightly controlled, the economical casting of a duelist who knew he had maybe three minutes of sustained combat left. Tomas's aegis was barely functional, the ram a ghost of itself, his own mana reserves scraping the bottom of what two weeks of cultivation could provide.

But neither of them yielded.

"Round three," Magister Brimstone said. "Begin."

Renn came forward this time. Not ranged bombardment — close-quarters combat, the Pyromancer's secondary specialty. His hands were sheathed in flame, turning his fists into weapons that could burn through leather and leave second-degree burns on contact. Tomas had no answer for this. The Sentinel Class was designed to hold position against ranged threats. Close-quarters was a weakness.

But he had the ram.

The totem couldn't match Renn's speed — it was a defender, not a striker — but it could position itself between Tomas and the worst of the blows. Renn's first punch connected with the ram's spectral shoulder instead of Tomas's ribs. The second was deflected by a horn-sweep. The third found Tomas's jaw — a glancing blow, but hard enough to make his vision blur — and Tomas understood that he was losing.

Then everything changed.

Renn stepped back, gathering mana for a final attack — something big, something that was meant to end the duel decisively — and Tomas felt the pressure in his chest spike. Not pain. Not exhaustion. Something else. Something the ram was trying to tell him.

*Danger. Real danger.*

He saw it at the same moment Magister Brimstone did. The mana Renn was gathering had crossed a threshold — not lethal intent, not quite, but a loss of control. The flame was feeding on something more than Renn's will, drawing ambient mana from the arena itself, growing beyond what the Pyromancer had intended. Renn's eyes went wide with alarm. He was trying to release it, trying to abort, but the spell had gained too much momentum and was now demanding completion.

The fireball that left Renn's hands was the size of a cartwheel and twice as fast as anything he'd thrown before.

Tomas didn't think. The ram didn't think. They moved together — a single impulse, a single action, the Sentinel and the totem acting as one being for the first time since Tomas's awakening. The ram stepped directly into the fireball's path, and Tomas stepped with it, and the aegis that formed around them both was not a wedge or a dome or a wall.

It was a sphere. Complete. Seamless. A bubble of silver light that enclosed them both, and when the fireball struck, it didn't deflect.

It broke.

The mana backlash hit Tomas like a physical blow — the cost of absorbing a spell that should have been lethal — and he went down. Hard. The ram went down with him, its form dissolving into motes of silver light that scattered across the arena floor like startled fireflies. Tomas lay on his back, staring at the sky, unable to move, unable to breathe, unable to feel anything except the vast, hollow emptiness where his mana had been.

But he was inside the circle. And he was alive.

"Match," Magister Brimstone said. Her voice was different now — not the formal cadence of an adjudicator but something sharper, more urgent. "Pyromancer, *control*. Sentinel, stay down. Medics."

Renn was kneeling beside him before the medics arrived. His face was ashen.

"I lost control," he said. "I could have killed you."

"You didn't."

"That wasn't me. That was you. You and the ram. You absorbed it."

"The ram did."

"The ram is you." Renn's voice cracked. "Saints, Herdsson. That spell would have killed anyone else in this cohort. I threw a lethal spell at a first-year. I threw a lethal spell and you're still breathing."

"Barely," Tomas said, and managed something that was almost a smile.

Magister Brimstone reached them, and her expression was the most complicated thing Tomas had ever seen on her face. Anger, concern, and something that might have been pride, all fighting for dominance.

"The duel is ruled a draw," she said. "Both combatants exceeded standard parameters. Both demonstrated capabilities beyond Apprentice level. Both will report to the infirmary immediately, and both will spend the next week in remedial control exercises." She looked at Renn. "You will learn to regulate your mana under stress before you throw another spell in my arena. Understood?"

"Yes, Magister."

"And you." She looked at Tomas. "Where did you learn to form a full aegis sphere? That's a Journeyman-level technique. I don't teach it until third year."

"I didn't learn it," Tomas said. "The ram did."

Magister Brimstone stared at him for a long moment. Then she shook her head — a small, almost imperceptible motion — and said something under her breath that Tomas didn't catch.

"Infirmary," she repeated. "Both of you. Now."

---

The infirmary was in the Dormitory Ring, a low stone building with windows that showed whatever sky the patient needed to see. Tomas's window showed a Veridale spring — grey sky, soft rain, the particular quality of light that meant lambing season was almost over. He lay in a bed that was not his, with a blanket that was not his, and waited for his mana to regenerate.

The ram was back. It had reappeared approximately two hours after the duel, materializing at the foot of Tomas's bed like a cat that had decided to forgive him. Its form was still translucent — the mana reserves would take days to fully recover — but it was present, watchful, its head turned toward the infirmary door.

"You almost died," Tomas said to it.

The ram did not reply.

"I almost died, and you threw yourself in front of a lethal spell, and I don't even know if you can die. Can you die? What happens to me if you die?"

The ram did not reply.

"Right," Tomas said. "Of course."

The infirmary door opened. Tomas expected a medic — the brisk Seed Division healer who had checked his mana levels an hour ago and pronounced him stable but depleted. What he got was Flynn Wintersbloom.

Flynn stood in the doorway with an expression that Tomas had never seen on his face before. It took Tomas a moment to identify it. Fear. Not the polite, distant concern of a classmate. Real fear, the kind that had been sitting in someone's chest for hours and was only now being released.

"You're alive," Flynn said.

"Last time I checked."

"I saw the duel." Flynn stepped into the room, and the door closed behind him with a soft click. "I saw the fireball. Everyone saw the fireball. The whole Training Ring went silent. For about ten seconds, everyone thought you were dead."

"I wasn't."

"I know. I saw the aegis. Everyone saw the aegis." Flynn stopped at the foot of the bed, his hands clenched at his sides. "You're an idiot. You're the biggest idiot I've ever met. You threw yourself in front of a lethal spell for a *duel*. A training duel. Points on a scoreboard. And you almost died."

"I didn't almost die."

"You did. The healer said your mana reserves dropped below the survival threshold for approximately six seconds. That's six seconds where your body was running on nothing. Six seconds where your heart could have stopped."

Tomas blinked. He hadn't known that. The healer hadn't mentioned it.

"Why are you here?" he asked.

Flynn's jaw tightened. "I don't know."

"Yes, you do."

"I don't." Flynn's voice was barely controlled. "I was in the library when the duel started. I told myself I wasn't going to watch. I told myself I didn't care. And then someone ran past shouting that the Pyromancer had thrown a death-bolt, and I was running before I knew I was running, and I got to the arena just in time to see you hit the ground with your ram dissolving around you like it was dying, and I thought—"

He stopped.

"What did you think?"

"I thought nobody else was going to stand in front of me."

The silence that followed was the most honest thing that had ever passed between them.

Tomas looked at Flynn — really looked, the way he looked at sheep when he was trying to figure out which one was sick. Flynn Wintersbloom was not sick. He was frightened. He was frightened of losing the only person at PactSpire who didn't perform for him, who didn't calculate his value in terms of his father's name, who had told him to stop pretending and waited to see what was underneath.

"Sit down," Tomas said.

Flynn sat in the chair beside the bed. His hands were still shaking. Tomas noticed, without comment, that Flynn's Quick-Cast was leaking — tiny sparks of mana flickering at his fingertips, the unconscious magic of a Cantrip Jester who hadn't learned to contain his emotions.

"I'm not going to die," Tomas said. "I'm very hard to kill. My father says it's my only talent."

"That's not true."

"It is, actually. I'm not smart. I'm not fast. I'm not charming. But I'm very, very hard to kill. It's the one thing I'm good at."

"That's not—" Flynn stopped. Took a breath. "You're good at other things."

"Name one."

"Making people feel safe."

Tomas considered this. The ram had moved closer to the bed — close enough that its spectral warmth pressed against Tomas's shoulder. It was watching Flynn with the same steady attention it gave everything, but there was something different in its posture. Tomas couldn't identify it. The totem's emotions were not his emotions. They were adjacent, overlapping, but never quite the same.

"That's not a talent," Tomas said. "That's just what the Class does."

"No." Flynn's voice was very quiet. "The Class makes you a protector. It doesn't make you someone people want to be protected by. That's you. That's separate."

Tomas didn't know what to say to that. He had never been good at receiving compliments, partly because he had received so few of them and partly because the ones he had received — about his strength, his stamina, his ability to carry things heavier than himself — had always felt like descriptions of livestock rather than people.

"Thank you," he said eventually.

"You're welcome."

"I mean for coming."

Flynn looked away. "I didn't plan to. I told you. I don't know why I'm here."

"I know why you're here."

Flynn didn't answer. Tomas didn't push. The ram settled its head on the edge of the bed, and the infirmary window shifted from grey to pale gold — the campus, Tomas realized, was responding to something. Possibly to him. Possibly to the strange, fragile, unnameable thing that had just happened between a shepherd and a noble in a room that smelled of healing herbs.

---

The news of the duel spread through PactSpire faster than the morning bell.

By the following afternoon, Tomas Herdsson was no longer the farm boy who had arrived with dirt on his boots and a sheep at his side. He was the Sentinel who had survived a lethal spell in his third week of training. The Sentinel whose totem had formed an aegis sphere — a technique most Journeymen couldn't manage — in the space between one heartbeat and the next. The Sentinel who had gotten up afterward and asked Renn Ashvale if he was all right.

Renn told that last part himself. It was, Tomas suspected, his way of apologizing.

The cohort noticed. All six of them, in their different ways, recalibrated their understanding of the Veridale shepherd.

Sera Thornwell, the oldest and quietest of the cohort, found Tomas in the dining hall that evening and sat down across from him without asking permission. She was tall for a Thornwood native — the frontier tended to produce people who were either very large or very small, with nothing in between — and her Terra-Mage senses gave her the unnerving habit of looking at people as if she could see the ground beneath their feet.

"You fought well," she said. It was the first time she had spoken to him directly.

"Thank you."

"I've seen a Calamity," she said. "Not a big one. A Spawn — about the size of a horse, with too many legs. It killed two people in my village before the Guild responder arrived. I was twelve. I hid in the root cellar and listened to them die."

Tomas didn't know what to say to that. He didn't think there was anything to say.

"The Guild responder was a Totemic Sentinel," Sera continued. "From Stormhollow. Not as strong as you. His totem was a badger. He held the Spawn in place for forty seconds while the Pyromancer team burned it. He was shaking the whole time. I could see it from the cellar window." She paused. "He survived. Barely. But the two people who died would have been four without him. Maybe five."

She looked at Tomas with eyes that had seen things most sixteen-year-olds had not.

"The ram is not for duels," she said. "The ram is for the moment when everything is about to go wrong and there's a person behind you who can't save themselves. That's what the Class is for. Don't waste it on scoreboards."

She stood and walked away before Tomas could respond.

Kael Highwind came next, bounding into the infirmary the following morning with his Storm-Hawk perched on his shoulder and an expression of unrestrained enthusiasm that Tomas found both exhausting and oddly endearing.

"Everyone's talking about you!" Kael announced. "The whole campus. They're saying you're some kind of prodigy. They're saying Magister Brimstone is going to advance you to the intermediate drills. They're saying—"

"They're saying a lot of things that aren't true," Tomas said.

"But the aegis sphere! That's a Journeyman technique. How did you do it?"

"I don't know. The ram did it."

"The ram is you."

"That's what everyone keeps telling me."

Kael grinned — a wide, uncomplicated expression that made him look about twelve years old. "This is going to be excellent. A Sentinel who can form aegis spheres and a Pyromancer who throws death-bolts by accident. Our cohort is going to be the most interesting one in years."

"Accidental death-bolts are not interesting."

"Everything is interesting if you look at it right." Kael's Storm-Hawk screeched — a sound that was apparently agreement — and Kael nodded solemnly. "Pip agrees. Pip says you're going to be important."

"Pip?"

"The hawk. She has opinions."

Tomas looked at the bird. The bird looked back with the particular intensity of a predator evaluating whether something was edible. "What kind of opinions?"

"Mostly about mice. But also about people. She likes you. She says you're steady."

The hawk screeched again. Kael translated: "She says Flynn Wintersbloom smells like mint and anxiety. She's not wrong."

Tomas filed this information away for later consideration.

---

Ivy Belladonna did not visit the infirmary. Ivy Belladonna was Flynn's problem, not Tomas's, and she spent the week after the duel in the Forge Division workshops, refining her rune-etched apparatus with the single-minded intensity of someone who had noticed that someone else was getting attention and was determined to outperform them in a more academically respectable fashion.

Wren Vesper did not visit the infirmary either, but Tomas noticed that the shadow in the corner of his room seemed slightly deeper than it had been before the duel, and occasionally — when he was half-asleep, or when the light was wrong — he thought he saw movement in the darkness that wasn't the ram.

The campus noticed all of this. The campus noticed everything. And in the way of living architecture that had been shaped by centuries of accumulated oaths, it began to arrange the pieces on its board with the patience of something that had all the time in the world.

---

On the fifth day after the duel, Tomas was cleared to return to training.

He walked to the Training Ring at dawn — the same hour he had woken every day in Veridale, because sheep did not respect academic schedules — and found Renn Ashvale already there. Alone. Practicing.

Renn was running through a series of control exercises: small flames, precisely shaped, held at exact temperatures for exact durations. It was remedial work, the kind of thing most Pyromancers learned before they were old enough to hold a sword, and Renn was doing it with the grim focus of someone who had been publicly humiliated and was determined to ensure it never happened again.

"You're early," Tomas said.

"So are you."

"I'm always early."

Renn released the flame he was holding — a perfect sphere, Tomas noticed, steady as glass — and turned to face him. His expression was complicated.

"I owe you an apology," Renn said. "Formally. The Drakengard way."

"In Drakengard, isn't the formal apology supposed to be delivered in front of witnesses?"

"Yes." Renn's jaw tightened. "But I thought you might prefer it without an audience."

Tomas considered this. "You're right. I would."

"Then I apologize. I lost control. I could have killed you. It doesn't matter that I didn't mean to. A Pyromancer who can't control their flame is a danger to everyone around them. I was a danger. I'm sorry."

"Accepted."

"That's it?"

"That's it." Tomas stepped into the training circle, and the ram materialized beside him — still slightly translucent, still recovering, but solid enough to cast a shadow. "You made a mistake. You're fixing it. That's more than most people do."

Renn stared at him. "You're very strange, Herdsson."

"I've been told."

"I mean it. You should be angry. You should want revenge. That's how this works. That's how every duel I've ever fought has ended — with the loser nursing a grudge and the winner nursing their pride. And you're just—" He gestured vaguely. "Not."

"I grew up on a sheep farm," Tomas said. "You spend sixteen years watching things die because you weren't fast enough or strong enough or smart enough to save them, and you learn something. Grudges don't keep the flock alive. Action does. You're taking action. That's enough."

Renn was silent for a long moment. Then he said, very quietly, "I want to learn from you."

"What?"

"Not combat. Not technique. That." Renn pointed at the ram. "That thing you do. The steadiness. The way you don't let things get under your skin. I've been training my whole life to be the strongest fighter in any room, and you walked in with a sheep and made me realize I don't know the first thing about standing my ground."

"You want me to teach you how to be steady."

"Yes."

Tomas looked at the ram. The ram looked back with an expression that might have been amusement.

"I can try," Tomas said. "But I should warn you — I don't know how I do it. It's not a technique. It's just... what I am."

"Then I'll learn by watching." Renn extended his hand. "Partners? Not rivals. Not anymore. I don't want to compete with you. I want to stand beside you."

Tomas took his hand. The ram made a sound that was not quite a bleat — something deeper, something that resonated in Tomas's chest like a second heartbeat.

"Partners," Tomas said.

And somewhere in the Spire, in a room that no student was allowed to enter, Headmaster Aldric Vowkeeper opened his eyes and smiled.

*End of Chapter Three*
