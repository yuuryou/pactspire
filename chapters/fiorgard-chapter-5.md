# Chapter Five: Pact-Planting

The morning of Pact-Planting dawned clear and cold — the last breath of winter before spring took hold in earnest. Tomas woke before the bell, as he always did, but this time there was no sheep to tend and no hearth to stoke. There was only the ram, standing in the corner, its spectral form sharper than it had ever been. The campus was feeding it, Tomas realized. The accumulated mana of centuries of oaths, rising to meet the ceremony.

He dressed carefully. The clothes his father had packed were clean but worn — the wool shirt had been mended twice, the trousers let out at the seams to accommodate the muscle he'd built over weeks of training. He looked, he thought, like exactly what he was: a shepherd who had wandered into an academy and refused to leave.

The ram looked at him with an expression that might have been approval.

"You look the same as always," Tomas said.

The ram did not reply, which was probably for the best.

---

The Grand Hall had been transformed.

Tomas had seen the Hall before — during registration, during the Apprentice Vow ceremony that had marked the start of term — but he had never seen it like this. The ceiling was open to the sky, which was a depth of blue that Tomas associated with early summer rather than early spring. The campus was showing its best face, or perhaps its oldest one: the sky that had been overhead when the first oaths were sworn, five centuries ago. The stars were beginning to fade, but a few lingered — bright points of light that Tomas recognized as the constellations his father had taught him to navigate by.

The floor was warm. Not metaphorically warm — physically warm, radiating heat through the soles of Tomas's boots. The living stone had risen to meet the ceremony, and every step he took sent a pulse of warmth up through his legs, as if the Hall itself was welcoming him.

Seven hundred students filled the tiered benches, arranged by Division and year. The first-years sat in the front rows, closest to the center stage where they would soon stand alone. Tomas found his place in the Sword Division section — Renn on his left, a girl from Thornwood whose Class Tomas hadn't learned on his right — and tried to remember how to breathe.

"It's just words," Renn murmured. "You've spoken words before."

"Not like these."

"No. Not like these."

Magister Brimstone was standing at the front of the Sword Division section, her expression unreadable. She caught Tomas's eye and held it for a moment — not a nod, not a smile, but something that felt like acknowledgment. Tomas didn't know what he'd done to earn it. Maybe surviving Renn's death-bolt was enough.

The Stage Division section was across the Hall, on the opposite side of the central stage. Tomas could see Flynn in the front row, seated beside Maestro Corvin Stagewright — the tall, angular Stage Division master whose hands never stopped moving, even when he was still. Flynn's expression was the same mask Tomas had seen during the opening ceremony: perfect composure, perfect posture, the Wintersbloom heir performing the role of someone who was not terrified.

But Tomas could see the cracks now. The way Flynn's hands were clasped too tightly in his lap. The way his Quick-Cast was leaking again — tiny sparks flickering at his knuckles, invisible to anyone who wasn't looking but bright as signal fires to Tomas. The ram had taught him to see these things. Or perhaps he had always seen them, and the ram had simply given him permission to acknowledge it.

The ceremony began.

---

Headmaster Aldric Vowkeeper walked to the center of the Hall, and the warmth of the floor intensified with every step he took.

He was an old man — older than Tomas's father, older than anyone Tomas had ever seen in Veridale. His hair was white and thin, his face lined with decades of something that was not quite worry and not quite grief. But his eyes were young. Bright. The eyes of someone who had seen the worst the world could offer and had decided, deliberately, to remain hopeful.

"Pact-Planting," he said, and his voice carried without amplification — the Hall itself projecting his words to every corner. "The ceremony of first oaths. The moment when a student becomes an Apprentice. The moment when the campus decides."

He paused. The silence was absolute.

"Five hundred and forty-seven years ago, the first Masters of PactSpire stood in this Hall and swore the Founders' Oath: to teach, to guard, to remember. The campus answered them. It grew from their words — stone from promise, warmth from commitment, life from the accumulated trust of every student who has ever stood where you stand now." He looked out at the first-year section. "Today, you will add your oaths to that trust. Some of you will be accepted. Some of you will not. I want you to understand that this is not a judgment of your worth. It is a judgment of your readiness. The campus knows what you can become. It also knows what you are now. If it asks you to leave, it is not rejecting you. It is protecting you — from an oath you are not ready to keep."

Tomas felt a chill that had nothing to do with the temperature. The warmth of the floor was still there, still real, but beneath it was something else: the awareness that this ceremony was not a formality. It was a test. And he had no idea whether he would pass.

"The ceremony will proceed by Division," Headmaster Vowkeeper continued. "When your Division is called, you will walk to the center of the Hall and speak your oath before your Grandmaster. The Pact Library will record your name, or it will not. The campus will accept you, or it will not. There is no partial acceptance. There is no second attempt. You speak once. The Hall answers once. That is all."

He stepped back, and the ceremony began.

---

The Seed Division went first. Tomas watched as the agricultural apprentices walked to the center of the Hall — farmers and herders and field-workers, their vows simple and practical and, in their own way, beautiful. One girl from Veridale swore to make the fields feed the hungry. A boy from Stormhollow swore to tame the winds that threatened the harvest. The Pact Library's book wrote their names. The floor warmed beneath them. The campus accepted.

Then the Forge Division. Ivy Belladonna walked to the center of the Hall with the rigid posture of someone who had rehearsed this moment for years. Her oath was precise, technical, almost clinical — a vow to forge tools that would serve the pact-bound kingdoms, to create rather than destroy, to build the infrastructure of civilization. It was not passionate. It was not moving. But it was absolutely sincere, and the campus recognized sincerity. The book wrote her name. The floor warmed. Ivy walked back to her seat with the faintest hint of a smile — the first Tomas had ever seen on her face.

The Frontier Division. Kael Highwind practically bounced to the center of the Hall, Pip circling overhead in defiance of every rule of indoor decorum. His oath was a ramble — something about sky and storm and the bond between beast and rider — but it was so utterly, transparently Kael that the campus accepted it without hesitation. The book wrote his name. Pip screeched in triumph. Kael walked back to his seat grinning, and several Masters smiled despite themselves.

The Veil Division. Wren Vesper walked to the center of the Hall with the same silence they brought to everything. Their oath was brief — a dozen words at most — but Tomas couldn't hear it. None of the first-years could. The Hall had chosen to keep it private, and that was a statement in itself: the campus recognized Wren's need for secrecy and accommodated it. The book wrote their name. The floor warmed. Wren walked back to their seat, and their expression was the most complicated thing Tomas had ever seen on a human face.

Then the Sword Division.

Renn went first. His oath was exactly what Tomas had expected — the Drakengard war-vow, spoken in the old language, flames flickering at his fingertips in unconscious punctuation. It was grand. It was fierce. It was everything a Pyromancer's oath should be. The book wrote his name. The floor flared so hot that Tomas could feel it from ten feet away. The campus accepted, and accepted strongly.

Then it was Tomas's turn.

He walked to the center of the Hall, and the ram walked beside him, and seven hundred students watched a shepherd with dirt on his boots try to find words for something he'd never said aloud.

Grandmaster Kestrel Ashford stood before him. Her scarred face was unreadable, but her eyes — those eyes that had seen things Tomas couldn't imagine — were warm.

"Speak," she said.

Tomas opened his mouth. Nothing came out.

He had thought about this moment. He had lain awake at night, staring at the ceiling of Hall Verdant, trying to craft the perfect oath. Something grand. Something worthy of the Sword Division. Something that would make the campus sit up and take notice. But every version he'd composed had felt wrong — too artificial, too performative, too much like the beautiful lies Flynn had learned to tell.

He was not Flynn.

"I don't have ancestral mana," he said, and the words came out rougher than he'd intended. "My family has never held a pact. My father tends sheep. His father tended sheep. As far back as anyone remembers, that's all we've done. I don't have a legacy to uphold or a tradition to continue. I have a ram who showed up on my sixteenth birthday and a piece of advice my father gave me before I left."

He paused. The Hall was silent. The ram stood motionless at his side.

"'If it protects you,' my father said, 'you protect it back.' That's the whole thing. Don't let anyone tell you different." Tomas looked at Grandmaster Ashford. "I don't know if that's enough for a Sword Division oath. I don't know if it's grand enough or fierce enough or traditional enough. But it's what I have. It's all I have. I swear to protect what protects me. I swear to stand in front of things that can't stand for themselves. I swear to be the wall. That's my oath. That's all of it."

Silence.

Then the floor warmed. Not the dramatic flare Renn had received — something quieter, steadier, a warmth that spread up through Tomas's boots and into his bones and stayed there. The Pact Library's book wrote his name in letters that glowed silver for a moment before settling into permanent ink.

The campus accepted.

Grandmaster Ashford stepped forward and placed a hand on Tomas's shoulder. "That," she said, barely above a whisper, "was the truest oath I've heard in twenty years of Pact-Plantings. Your father was right. Don't let anyone tell you different."

Tomas walked back to his seat, and the ram walked beside him, and for the first time since his awakening, he felt like he belonged exactly where he was.

---

The Stage Division was called last.

Flynn stood. Maestro Stagewright caught his eye and gave him a single nod — encouragement and expectation and challenge, all compressed into one economical gesture. Flynn walked to the center of the Hall, and his mind was blank.

He had composed a dozen oaths over the past three days. Beautiful oaths. Clever oaths. Oaths full of wordplay and theatrical flair and the kind of rhetorical sophistication that would have made his ancestors proud. He had thrown them all away. They were performances — excellent performances, the kind any Stage master would applaud — and that was exactly the problem.

The Hall was waiting. Seven hundred students. The Masters. The Headmaster. The campus itself, which was listening in a way that buildings did not usually listen.

Flynn opened his mouth, and the words that came out were not the ones he had planned.

"My family has seventeen potion patents," he said. "My grandfather was Arch-Apothecarian of Miravale for forty-seven years. I was supposed to be an Apothecarian. I was supposed to continue the legacy. I was supposed to make my father proud."

He paused. The Hall was very still.

"I'm not an Apothecarian. I'm a Cantrip Jester. The Class that my family considers beneath our name. The Class that makes my father close his study door and refuse to speak to me. The Class that means I will never sign a patent or carry the weight of seventeen ancestral achievements."

His voice cracked — a tiny fracture, barely perceptible, but in the silence of the Hall it rang like a bell.

"I don't know why the world gave me this Class. I don't know what I'm supposed to do with it. But I know that when I'm improvising — when I'm not planning, not preparing, not performing — I feel more like myself than I ever did in my father's laboratory. I know that Quick-Cast isn't just a trick. It's a way of being in the world that doesn't require me to be someone else first." He took a breath. "I swear to find out what a Cantrip Jester can do that no one has thought of yet. I swear to make the Class my own, even if my family never understands. I swear to stop performing and start being. That's my oath. That's all I have."

The silence stretched.

Then the floor warmed — a slow, spreading heat that felt nothing like the dramatic flare Renn had received. It was gentler. Steadier. The warmth of a fire that was still learning how to burn but had finally found its fuel.

The Pact Library's book wrote his name. The letters glowed prismatic — a kaleidoscope of color, fracturing and reforming, refusing to settle — before coalescing into silver ink.

Maestro Stagewright was smiling. It was, Flynn realized, the first time he had seen his mentor truly smile.

"Well done," Stagewright murmured. "Well done, Wintersbloom."

Flynn walked back to his seat, and his hands were shaking, and he didn't care. Across the Hall, in the Sword Division section, Tomas Herdsson was watching him with an expression that Flynn couldn't read.

But he could feel it. Through something that wasn't quite a link — not yet, not yet — but was adjacent to one. A warmth. A recognition.

The shepherd had told the truth. The noble had told the truth. And somewhere in the Hall, in the space between their two truths, something new was beginning to grow.

---

That night, the campus celebrated.

The Pact-Planting festival was the first major ceremony of the academic year, and it was observed with food and music and a temporary suspension of the usual rules about curfew and quiet hours. The Grand Hall transformed from a solemn chamber into a festival ground — long tables appeared from nowhere, laden with dishes from all seven kingdoms. Lanterns drifted through the air like slow-moving stars. The ceiling showed a clear Veridale spring night, full of stars that Tomas recognized, and the warmth of the floor was now a pleasant background hum rather than the intense pulse of the ceremony.

The cohort gathered at a table near the eastern wall — all seven of them, for the first time in a social rather than training context. Kael had procured an alarming quantity of Drakengard fire-wine, which he was distributing with the enthusiasm of someone who had not yet learned that alcohol and Pyromancy did not mix. Pip had discovered the roast meat table and was defending it from all comers with the territorial ferocity of a much larger bird.

"We all passed," Kael said, raising his cup. "Every single one of us. The campus accepted the whole cohort. Do you know how rare that is?"

"About one in four cohorts passes intact," Ivy said. "The statistical probability of seven individual acceptances without a single rejection is approximately—"

"Nobody cares about the math," Renn said.

"I care about the math."

"The math doesn't care about you."

Ivy made a sound of frustration and turned to Sera, who was the only person at the table who seemed willing to engage with her on academic matters. Sera, for her part, was nursing a single cup of fire-wine and watching the festival with the quiet watchfulness of someone who had never attended a celebration where she wasn't expecting an attack.

"You're not drinking," Tomas observed.

"I don't drink at celebrations. Too many memories."

"Of what?"

Sera was silent for a moment. Then: "The first time a Calamity attacked my village, it was during a festival. A harvest celebration. Everyone was drunk. Nobody heard it coming. Twelve people died before the Guild responder arrived."

The table went quiet. Kael lowered his cup. Pip, sensing the shift in atmosphere, abandoned the roast meat table and flew back to Kael's shoulder.

"I'm sorry," Tomas said.

"Don't be. It was a long time ago." Sera's voice was steady. "But I learned something that night. Celebrations are when people let their guard down. And things that want to hurt you know that."

"Is that why you're always watching?" Flynn asked.

"Yes."

"Does it help?"

Sera looked at him — really looked, the way she looked at people when she was deciding whether they were worth the effort of honesty. "Sometimes. Most of the time, it just means I see the thing coming before everyone else. It doesn't stop it."

Flynn didn't know what to say to that. Across the table, Tomas caught his eye — a look that said, without words, *let her be*. Flynn nodded and looked away.

Wren, who had been silent throughout the entire celebration, said: "The Calamity that attacked your village. Was it drawn by the festival?"

Sera's eyes narrowed. "What do you mean?"

"Magister Thorne says Calamities are attracted to pact energy. The stronger the pact activity in an area, the more likely a Spawn is to manifest. A harvest festival, with all the accumulated agricultural pacts reaching their peak..." Wren trailed off.

"That's not something Magister Thorne teaches in class," Ivy said sharply. "That's from the restricted texts."

Wren's expression didn't change. "I read broadly."

"You read what Magister Thorne gives you."

"Is there a difference?"

The tension at the table had solidified into something almost physical. Tomas could feel the ram tensing beside him, responding to the shift in the air.

"Magister Thorne has ideas about the pact system," Tomas said, carefully. "Some of those ideas are... unusual."

"Unusual isn't the same as wrong," Wren said.

"No. But it's not the same as right, either."

Wren looked at Tomas for a long moment. Then, so quietly that only Tomas could hear: "You're worried about me."

"You're in our cohort."

"That's not an answer."

"It's the only one I have."

Wren's lips twitched — the ghost of an expression that might have been amusement or might have been something sadder. "You're very strange, Herdsson."

"I've been told."

---

The festival continued. The fire-wine flowed. At some point, Kael challenged Renn to a drinking contest, which Renn accepted with Drakengard pride and promptly lost, because Pyromancers burned through alcohol twice as fast as Beast-Tamers and Kael had not bothered to mention this before issuing the challenge. Pip got into the roast meat table again. Ivy produced a notebook and began documenting the effects of fire-wine on mana cultivation, because Ivy could not turn off her academic instincts even during a party.

Sera stayed until midnight and then slipped away without saying goodbye — the Thornwood way, Tomas had learned, which meant you left when you were done and trusted the people who mattered to understand.

Wren stayed longer, watching everything with those pale eyes that reflected no light. At some point, they caught Tomas looking at them and held his gaze for a long, evaluating moment. Then they nodded — once, barely perceptible — and left.

Tomas didn't know what the nod meant. He suspected it might be important.

Flynn stayed until nearly dawn.

They found themselves alone at the table, the food gone, the lanterns dimming, the other students drifting back to their dormitories in twos and threes. The ceiling had shifted from Veridale spring to Miravale dawn — a sky of pale gold and rose, the colors Flynn had grown up watching from his window.

"My father didn't come," Flynn said.

Tomas looked at him. "You expected him to?"

"No. Yes. I don't know." Flynn's voice was tired — more honest than he usually allowed himself to be. "I thought maybe, if I swore a good enough oath... if the campus accepted me strongly enough... he might..."

"Forgive you for not being what he wanted?"

Flynn closed his eyes. "Something like that."

"Your oath wasn't for him," Tomas said. "It was for you."

"I know."

"Do you?"

Flynn opened his eyes. The Miravale dawn was fading into full morning, and the Hall was beginning to stir with the presence of servants clearing the tables and resetting the space for the day's activities. But for a moment longer, it was just the two of them — the shepherd and the noble, the Sentinel and the Jester, two people who had told the truth in a room full of performances.

"No," Flynn said quietly. "But I'm learning."

The ram, which had been standing guard at Tomas's side all night, made a sound that was not quite a bleat. It was almost, Tomas thought, like approval.

*End of Chapter Five*
