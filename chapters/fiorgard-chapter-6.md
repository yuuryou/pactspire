# Chapter Six: Field Exercise

The field exercise was supposed to be routine.

Twice a term, the first-year cohort was taken beyond the campus walls for practical training in uncontrolled environments. The stated purpose was acclimation — learning to cast under pressure, learning to cooperate, learning to apply classroom theory in situations where the stakes were real and the adjudicator was not a faculty member but the world itself. The unstated purpose was assessment. The Masters used field exercises to evaluate the cohort's dynamics, to identify strengths and flaws before they mattered in combat, to see who would lead and who would follow and who would stand alone.

Tomas had been looking forward to it for weeks. The campus was safe. The campus was warm. The campus meant well. But the campus was also a cage — a beautiful, living cage that responded to every emotion and rearranged itself around every need. Tomas had spent sixteen years outdoors, and the walls were beginning to press on him in ways he hadn't anticipated.

"Today," Magister Brimstone announced, as the cohort assembled at the North Gate at dawn, "you will navigate the Thornwood boundary zone. Four miles of mixed terrain — forest, stream, and the edge of the old quarry. Your objective is the boundary marker at the northernmost point of PactSpire's territory. You will travel as a unit. You will face simulated threats. You will respond according to your training. Any questions?"

"What kind of threats?" Kael asked.

"The kind that test your formation."

"That's not an answer."

"It's the only one you're getting." Magister Brimstone's expression was flat. "This is not a classroom exercise. This is a field deployment. The threats will be appropriate to your skill level and lethal precisely insofar as you fail to meet them. Do not fail."

The North Gate opened, and the seven of them stepped through.

The Thornwood boundary zone was beautiful in the way that frontier landscapes were always beautiful — rough, untamed, full of shadows that were just deep enough to hide something dangerous. Tomas felt the ram tense as they crossed the threshold, its spectral senses reaching out to map terrain that no pact had ever touched. The campus's influence ended at the boundary line. Beyond it, the land was wild, and the pact magic that sustained PactSpire was something you carried, not something that surrounded you.

"Formation," Renn said, and his voice had the particular authority of someone who had trained for command since childhood. "Standard defensive wedge. Sera on point — you're the only one who can read the ground. Tomas behind her, aegis forward. Me at center — I have the widest threat radius. Kael, aerial recon. Fly high enough to see but not so high you attract attention. Ivy and Flynn, center-rear. Wren, flank guard. Questions?"

No one had questions. The wedge formed with the smoothness of a drill they had practiced twenty times — not perfectly, but well enough that Tomas felt a small pulse of pride. The cohort was becoming a unit, whether Ivy liked it or not.

They moved into the forest.

---

The first hour was quiet.

Thornwood was not dangerous in the way that the Abolished Kingdoms were dangerous. The Calamity incursions here were rare — once every few years, a Spawn would drift down from the Fractured North and be dispatched by a Guild patrol before it reached the border villages. But rare was not never. And the boundary zone was, by definition, the place where PactSpire's protection ended and the wild began.

Tomas kept the aegis angled forward, a shallow wedge that would deflect rather than absorb. The ram was alert, its head tracking sounds Tomas couldn't hear, its posture communicating something between vigilance and curiosity. It had never been this far from the campus. It was learning.

"The ground here is old rock," Sera said, her voice carrying back without turning. "Pre-Oath. No pact residue. The roots don't respond to Terra-Mage signals the way they do inside the boundary."

"Is that bad?" Kael called from overhead.

"It means I can't feel what's coming."

"Comforting."

"Comfort wasn't promised."

The forest grew denser. The sunlight, which had been bright at the boundary line, dimmed to a green-gold haze that made shadows pool between the trees. Tomas felt the ram's attention sharpen — not alarm, but something adjacent to it. There was something ahead. Something that didn't belong.

"Hold," Tomas said.

The wedge stopped. Renn turned, his hands already warming. "What?"

"Ram feels something. Fifty feet ahead, left of the stream."

Sera crouched, one hand pressed to the ground. "I can't feel anything. It might not be in the ground."

"Then it's in the trees."

Kael's Storm-Hawk screeched — a sharp, warning cry. Kael called down: "Movement. Something moving against the wind. Pip says it doesn't smell like animal."

Flynn's hands were sparking. "What does it smell like?"

"Wrong."

The first Spawn came through the trees at the speed of a galloping horse.

It was horse-sized, as Sera had described, but the comparison ended there. Six legs, articulated wrong. A body that seemed too long and too short at the same time. A head that was mostly mouth, and eyes that didn't reflect light. It moved like a spider that had decided to be a wolf, and the sound it made was not a roar or a howl but a high, thin keening that seemed to come from everywhere at once.

"Contact front!" Renn shouted, but Tomas was already moving.

The ram flared its aegis — not the angled wedge of the training drills but the full sphere Tomas had learned during the duel. The Spawn hit the barrier at full speed and bounced, skidding sideways with a screech of something that was not quite pain and not quite anger. But the impact was harder than anything Tomas had absorbed in training — harder than Renn's firebolts, harder than the practice constructs the Masters used. The aegis held, but Tomas felt the mana drain like a physical wound, a hollow ache that spread through his chest and arms.

"It's strong," he managed. "I can't hold the sphere for long."

"Then don't. Sera, roots!"

Sera's Terra-Mage training was not combat-focused, but frontier life taught skills that curriculum did not. She slammed her hands into the ground, and the old rock — pre-Oath, pact-resistant — resisted for a heartbeat before yielding. Roots erupted from the forest floor, not the controlled bindings of a practiced technique but a wild, desperate growth that wrapped around the Spawn's legs and held.

"Now!" Renn called.

The firebolt that left his hands was not the controlled flame of the training drills. It was war-fire — the Drakengard pyromantic tradition, fire that was meant to kill and kill thoroughly. The Spawn shrieked as the flames caught its flank, and the smell that rose was not burning flesh but burning absence — a negative scent, an anti-smell, the odor of something that should not exist being forced to stop existing.

But it didn't stop.

The Spawn tore through Sera's roots with a convulsion of limbs and lunged — not at Renn, who had hurt it, but at Tomas, who had stopped it. The aegis sphere flickered. Tomas's mana reserves, which had barely recovered from the Pact-Planting, were scraping bottom. The ram was translucent, struggling, its form dissolving at the edges.

And then the Spawn stopped.

Not because of the aegis. Not because of Sera's roots or Renn's fire. It stopped because the world around it had suddenly become wrong in a way that even a Calamity could not parse.

Flynn Wintersbloom stood at the center of the formation, his hands raised, his eyes wide, his Quick-Cast bleeding mana in quantities that should have been impossible for a first-year. The forest around the Spawn had been replaced — not by an illusion, not by a suggestion, but by a full, immersive, impossible Stage-working. The trees were now made of glass. The ground was now made of mirrors. The Spawn's own reflection surrounded it on all sides — six-legged and wrong and suddenly uncertain.

It was not a combat technique. It was not any technique Flynn had been taught. It was pure improvisation — the Jester's gift, deployed in desperation, and it was working.

"It can't find us," Flynn said. His voice was strained. "It can't find anything. The reflections are confusing it. But I can't hold this. I've never held anything this big."

"Renn, finish it," Tomas said.

Renn gathered his mana. His hands glowed white-hot — the temperature of someone who had learned, in the weeks since the duel, exactly where his control failed. He held the flame for three heartbeats, four, five — and then released.

The firebolt struck the Spawn's center mass and kept going. The creature's keening rose to a pitch that made Tomas's eardrums ache and then cut off entirely. The Spawn dissolved — not into ash, not into smoke, but into a fine, dark mist that rose into the canopy and vanished.

Silence.

Then Kael's voice, from overhead: "There's another one. Two hundred yards north. Moving fast."

---

The second Spawn was larger.

It came through the trees with the momentum of something that had already killed and expected to kill again. Tomas's aegis was barely functional — the ram was a ghost of itself, and Tomas's own mana reserves were depleted past the point of safe casting. Renn's flames were sputtering, the cost of the kill-shot having drained him nearly as badly as the aegis had drained Tomas. Flynn's illusion had collapsed, and he was on his knees, gasping, his Quick-Cast sparking in erratic, uncontrolled bursts.

Only Wren was untouched.

The Shadow-Mage stepped forward from flank guard with the particular silence that was not quite invisibility. The Spawn did not notice them — Shadow-Mages were hard for Calamities to notice, because what they did with light was not quite light and what they did with darkness was not quite darkness, and the Spawn's senses had never evolved to detect things that existed in the space between.

"The eyes," Wren said, quiet and precise. "Sera said they don't reflect light. That means they absorb it. If I create a void around its head — a space where no light exists at all — it will be blind."

"Do it," Renn said.

The shadow that Wren summoned was not the soft darkness of a moonless night. It was the absolute void of the Vanishers' Haze — the signature technique of Vespermarch, the shadow so deep that it didn't just block light but canceled it. The Spawn's head disappeared into a sphere of perfect, lightless darkness, and the creature stopped.

Not stopped moving. Stopped existing in a way that mattered. Its senses, which relied on light absorption, were receiving nothing. Its coordination, which relied on visual confirmation, was gone. It stumbled, its six legs tangling, its body crashing into trees with the blind, panicked momentum of something that had lost its primary interface with the world.

"Ivy, now," Sera said. "The apparatus!"

Ivy Belladonna had not been idle during the fight. Her rune-etched notebook was open, and the compact apparatus that was always strapped to her left arm — a contraption of brass and crystal and etched silver that Tomas had never understood — was glowing with the particular intensity of fully charged pact mana. The Forge Division didn't teach combat techniques. They taught creation. And creation, properly applied, could be the most devastating weapon of all.

The apparatus released a wave of sound — not noise, not music, but a precise frequency that resonated with the Spawn's crystalline structure and made it vibrate. The vibration became a shudder. The shudder became a fracture. The Spawn's body, which was not quite matter in the way that matter was supposed to be matter, began to crack along geometric lines that followed no natural pattern.

"Finish it," Ivy said. Her voice was calm. "It's structurally compromised. Any Class technique will work now."

Renn raised his hands. No flame came. He had nothing left.

Sera's roots were spent. Ivy's apparatus was glowing red-hot, seconds from catastrophic overload. Kael was still overhead, and his Storm-Bonded abilities were useless against a ground target. Wren's void could blind but not kill. Flynn was on his knees, barely conscious.

The Spawn, blind but still moving, still dangerous, still wrong, oriented on the sound of Ivy's voice.

"Tomas," Renn said.

"I don't have anything left."

"You do." Flynn's voice was a thread. "You have the ram. The ram is you. You told me—"

"That was before I burned through my reserves."

"Then don't use your reserves." Flynn pushed himself to his feet, and the effort it took was visible in every line of his body. "Use mine."

The words hit Tomas like a physical blow. "What?"

"I have mana. More than I can use right now. The Cantrip Jester's curse — fast recovery, low output. You have a Sentinel's output." Flynn's eyes met Tomas's, and there was no performance in them. There was nothing but desperate, naked honesty. "Take it. Whatever you need."

"That's not how pacts work."

"It could be. If we make it work."

The Spawn was orienting — its legs finding purchase, its blind head swinging toward them. Thirty feet. Twenty. Close enough that Tomas could see the texture of its not-skin, the way it seemed to absorb the forest's dim light rather than reflect it.

"Tomas," Flynn said, and his name was a plea.

Tomas reached out. Not with his hands — with the ram. The totem, which had been a fading wraith, flared silver as it touched Flynn's Quick-Cast spark. Mana flowed between them — not a proper pact, not a formal bond, but an emergency measure, a provisional connection that felt like lightning and grew like roots and held like nothing Tomas had ever experienced.

The aegis sphere reformed. But it was different now — brighter, sharper, layered with something that was not quite Sentinel magic and not quite Stage craft but a fusion of both. The ram stood at the center of the sphere, and its horns were prismatic, fracturing light the way Flynn's name had fractured in the Pact Library's book.

The Spawn hit the sphere and shattered.

Not deflected. Not absorbed. Shattered — its not-body dissolving into a fine spray of darkness that twisted upward and was gone before it touched the canopy.

Tomas dropped to his knees. Flynn dropped beside him. The sphere collapsed, and the ram flickered once before vanishing entirely — not dead, Tomas knew, but depleted. Resting. The way it had rested after the duel, but deeper this time. Closer to the edge.

The forest was quiet.

Sera was the first to speak. "What was that?"

"I don't know," Tomas said.

"Yes, you do. That wasn't a Sentinel technique. That wasn't any technique in the curriculum. That was something new."

Tomas looked at Flynn. Flynn looked back, and his expression was the most complicated thing Tomas had ever seen on his face — fear and exhaustion and something that looked almost like wonder.

"We made a pact," Flynn said. "Temporary. Emergency. It's not supposed to be possible without a formal Oath-binding."

"Apparently it is."

"The Masters are going to have questions."

"Let them ask."

Flynn laughed — a short, shaky sound that was half relief and half terror. "You're the most reckless person I've ever met."

"I've been told."

---

The third Spawn never came.

Magister Brimstone appeared at the boundary marker ten minutes later — not summoned, not alerted, but present with the particular authority of someone who had known exactly what was happening and had waited to see how the cohort would respond. Her expression, when she saw the dissolving residue of two Spawn on the forest floor, was utterly unreadable.

"Report," she said.

Renn gave the report — the Drakengard way, concise and factual — while Tomas sat on a fallen log and tried to remember how to breathe. The ram was still absent, and the absence felt like a missing limb. Flynn was beside him, closer than Tomas would normally have allowed, their shoulders almost touching. Neither of them moved.

When Renn finished, Magister Brimstone was silent for a long moment. Then she walked to where Tomas and Flynn were sitting and looked down at them.

"A provisional combat pact," she said. "Forged in the field, without a teacher, without a binding ceremony, without the Pact Library's certification. Do you understand how rare that is?"

"No," Tomas said.

"It's happened three times in PactSpire's recorded history. Each time, the students involved went on to become Masters. One of them is currently Headmaster Vowkeeper." She paused. "The campus will be informed. The Masters will want to study this. And you two—" She looked from Tomas to Flynn and back. "—are going to have a very difficult few terms ahead of you. People will want things from you. Attention you didn't ask for. Expectations you can't meet. I suggest you learn to rely on each other now, because the alternative is being pulled apart by everyone else."

She turned and walked back toward the formation, leaving Tomas and Flynn alone on the log.

"Three times in recorded history," Flynn said.

"I heard."

"That's either very good or very bad."

"It's probably both."

The forest was quiet. The sun was high now, filtering through the canopy in patches of gold that made the Spawn residue look almost beautiful. Somewhere overhead, Pip was circling, and Kael's distant voice was giving what sounded like a highly dramatic recounting of the battle to no one in particular.

"I gave you my mana," Flynn said. "I didn't even think about it. I just... did it."

"I know."

"Why?"

"Because you trusted me."

Flynn was silent for a moment. Then: "I don't trust anyone."

"You trusted me."

"I know." Flynn's voice was barely audible. "I don't understand it either."

The ram flickered back into existence beside Tomas — a pale, translucent version of itself, barely solid enough to cast a shadow. It looked at Flynn, and its expression was not hostile. It was something Tomas had never seen on the totem's face before. Something almost like recognition.

"We should get back to the campus," Tomas said.

"Yes."

"Magister Brimstone is going to make us write a report."

"Almost certainly."

"It's going to be very long."

"Almost certainly."

Tomas stood. Flynn stood. The ram flickered and steadied, and the seven of them — battered, exhausted, and suddenly, irrevocably linked — began the walk back to PactSpire.

*End of Chapter Six*
