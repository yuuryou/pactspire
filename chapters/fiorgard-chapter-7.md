# Chapter Seven: Forced Proximity

The provisional combat pact between Tomas Herdsson and Flynn Wintersbloom became, in the weeks following the field exercise, the most discussed topic on campus.

The Masters were fascinated. Maestro Stagewright requested daily reports on Flynn's mana integration patterns, his Quick-Cast stability, and any subjective impressions of the bond's effect on his Class abilities. Grandmaster Ashford summoned Tomas to the Sword Division offices three times in the first week alone, asking questions Tomas couldn't answer about how the ram had facilitated the connection and why the aegis sphere had manifested prismatic properties instead of the standard silver. Magister Brimstone observed their training sessions with an intensity that suggested she was taking notes for a monograph. Even Headmaster Vowkeeper, who rarely appeared outside of formal ceremonies, was seen walking the Training Ring perimeter during their practice hours, his expression thoughtful.

"Three times in five centuries," Kael said, for approximately the fifteenth time. "Do you understand how incredible that is? You're literally making history. Both of you. At the same time."

"It's not that incredible," Tomas said.

"It is literally the definition of incredible."

"It's inconvenient," Flynn said. "People keep staring at me in the library. I can't study without someone 'accidentally' sitting at my table."

"People have always stared at you in the library," Ivy said.

"Yes, but now they're staring *differently*."

The seven of them were in the common room of Hall Verdant — the rough-hewn frontier dormitory that Tomas had grown attached to despite everything — and the evening fire was casting long shadows across the walls. Wren was in their usual corner, ostensibly reading but actually watching. Sera was mending a tear in her training tunic, her stitches so precise they might have been runic. Renn was polishing a practice blade. Kael was feeding Pip strips of dried meat. Ivy was taking notes.

It was, Tomas realized, the closest thing to a family he had ever had outside of Veridale.

"The Masters want us to train together," he said. "Formally. Every day. Combat Fundamentals and individual practice, until they understand how the bond works."

"Every day?" Flynn's voice was flat.

"Every day."

"For how long?"

"Indefinitely."

Flynn stared at him. Tomas stared back. The ram, which was standing at Tomas's side as always, made a sound that was definitely amusement.

---

The forced training sessions were held in a private chamber adjacent to the Training Ring — a space the Masters had apparently created specifically for this purpose, because the campus responded to need before anyone had to ask. It was smaller than the main arena, maybe twenty feet across, with walls of pale grey stone that absorbed mana signatures for later analysis. The Masters, Tomas gathered, were treating them as a research project as much as students.

"Again," Magister Brimstone said.

It was the third day of combined training, and Tomas was exhausted. Not physically — his Sentinel stamina was better than it had ever been — but mentally. The bond between himself and Flynn was not a simple thing. It was a channel that flowed both ways, and every time they connected, Tomas caught glimpses of things he wasn't supposed to see. Flynn's anxiety. Flynn's fear of failure. The constant, grinding pressure of seventeen ancestral patents and a father who had not written since the Apprentice Vow.

And Flynn, presumably, was catching glimpses of Tomas in return. The sheep farm. The loneliness. The quiet, abiding terror of being the first Herdsson in recorded history to do anything that anyone would record.

They didn't talk about it. It was easier not to.

"Again," Magister Brimstone repeated.

Tomas raised the aegis — the standard version, the simple wedge. Flynn reached out with his Quick-Cast, and the connection snapped into place like a lock closing. The aegis flared prismatic, fracturing light into a dozen colors, and Tomas felt the familiar dual-pulse of his own mana and Flynn's supplying the structure.

"Good," Magister Brimstone said. "Now hold it."

Holding was harder than forming. The bond wanted to fluctuate — Flynn's mana was fast and volatile, Tomas's was slow and steady, and synchronizing them was like trying to keep a candle flame and a campfire at exactly the same height. Tomas felt the connection waver. Flynn adjusted. The aegis steadied.

"Better. Again."

They trained for three hours. By the end, Tomas was barely standing, and Flynn was lying flat on his back on the stone floor, staring at the ceiling with the particular exhaustion of someone whose mana had been drained and refilled and drained again until the reserves themselves were raw.

"You're heavy," Flynn said.

"I'm not heavy. You're light."

"That's the same thing."

"It's not."

Flynn made a sound that was halfway between a laugh and a groan. "You're the most literal person I have ever met."

"I've been told."

---

The thing about forced proximity, Tomas learned, was that it forced honesty.

Not the performative honesty of a formal confession or the careful honesty of a diplomatic negotiation. The kind of honesty that happens when two people are too tired to maintain their walls, and the bond between them is leaking fragments of thought and feeling without either of them meaning to share.

On the fifth day, Flynn said: "You're afraid of something."

They were resting between drills — a rare five-minute break that Magister Brimstone had reluctantly granted. Tomas was sitting with his back against the wall, the ram settled beside him. Flynn was across the chamber, closer than he would have been a month ago but still maintaining a careful distance.

"Everyone's afraid of something," Tomas said.

"Yours is specific. I can feel it through the bond. It's like a cold spot. Something you don't want to think about."

Tomas was silent.

"It's about the ram," Flynn said. "About what happens if you fail."

"I'm not going to fail."

"That's not what the fear is. The fear is that you will fail and someone else will pay for it."

Tomas looked at him. The ram looked at him. The chamber, which had been recording their mana signatures with academic precision, was suddenly very quiet.

"I grew up on a sheep farm," Tomas said. "Things die on sheep farms. Lambs die. Ewes die. Sometimes the whole flock gets sick, and there's nothing you can do except watch. My father taught me to accept it — to do what you can and let go of the rest. But I never learned the second part. I can't let go. I can't watch something die and accept that it was inevitable."

"So you carry it."

"Everyone carries something."

Flynn was quiet for a moment. Then: "My father hasn't written. Not since the Apprentice Vow. Not since I told him I wasn't going to be an Apothecarian. My mother writes — twice a week, letters full of pleasantries that don't say anything about how he's doing or whether he's mentioned my name. She's trying to protect me. But I know what it means. He's waiting for me to fail. He's waiting for me to come home and say I was wrong, that the Cantrip Jester class was a mistake, that I'm ready to be his heir after all."

"And are you?"

"No." Flynn's voice cracked. "That's the worst part. I know I'm disappointing him. I know I'm breaking something that might never be fixed. And I still don't want to go back."

The bond between them pulsed — a moment of perfect alignment, two fears resonating at the same frequency. Tomas didn't know what to say. He had never been good with words, and the words that came to mind felt insufficient for the weight of what Flynn had shared.

"My father said one other thing," Tomas said eventually. "Before I left. He said, 'The ram chose you. It didn't choose your grandfather or your great-grandfather or any Herdsson that came before. It chose you. That means you're the one who decides what it means, not them.'"

Flynn looked at him. "Are you telling me the Cantrip Jester class chose me?"

"I'm telling you that you're the one who decides what it means."

The silence that followed was different from the ones before. It was not uncomfortable. It was a silence of two people who had each seen something private in the other and had decided, simultaneously, not to look away.

Magister Brimstone returned from wherever she had been watching — Tomas suspected the chamber was observed, though he'd never found the observation point — and the training resumed. But something had shifted. The bond was easier to hold. The synchronization was smoother. As if the honesty had cleared a channel that had been blocked before.

---

The campus noticed the shift.

Hallways rearranged themselves so that Tomas and Flynn kept running into each other between classes. The Pact Library, which had previously tolerated Tomas's presence with the neutral indifference of a building that had seen centuries of students come and go, began to warm when he entered — not the dramatic pulse of the Pact-Planting ceremony, but a steady, welcoming heat that felt like approval. The Fracture Line in the central courtyard pulsed more brightly at night, and Tomas could have sworn the rhythm matched his heartbeat.

"The campus is matchmaking," Kael said, watching the Fracture Line from the common room window. "It did this with my parents. My mother was a Beast-Tamer, my father was a Terra-Mage. The campus kept putting them in the same rotation groups until they stopped fighting and started cooperating."

"The campus doesn't matchmake," Ivy said.

"It literally does. Maestro Stagewright wrote a monograph about it."

"The monograph was speculative."

"All monographs are speculative. That's what monographs are."

Ivy made a sound of frustration and returned to her notes. But Tomas noticed that she didn't argue further.

Wren, who had been silent throughout the exchange, said: "The campus is a pact itself. It responds to bonds. Strong bonds create resonance. Resonance changes the architecture."

Everyone turned to look at them.

"That's from the restricted texts," Ivy said.

"It's from observation," Wren said, "which is free."

---

The combined training continued. Two weeks. Three. The bond between Tomas and Flynn grew steadier, more reliable, until they could form the prismatic aegis sphere in under three seconds and hold it for nearly a minute without fluctuation. Magister Brimstone's expression, which had been skeptical at first, shifted to something that was almost impressed.

But it was the fourth week that changed everything.

They were running a new drill — Magister Brimstone had constructed a practice construct that mimicked the Spawn they had fought in the forest. It was a clever piece of work: not truly dangerous, but convincing enough to trigger the body's threat responses. The construct lunged, and Tomas raised the sphere, and Flynn fed it mana, and the drill was proceeding exactly as planned.

And then Tomas felt it.

Not through the aegis. Through the bond. A surge of emotion so strong and so sudden that it overwhelmed the channel between them: terror, pure and undiluted, the terror of someone who had seen something that broke the world. It was not Tomas's terror. It was Flynn's.

The construct hit the sphere, but Tomas barely noticed. He was turning, reaching for Flynn, the ram already moving to intercept whatever threat had caused the reaction. But there was no threat. There was only Flynn, standing motionless in the center of the chamber, his face ashen, his Quick-Cast sparking wildly.

"What?" Tomas said. "What happened?"

Flynn didn't answer. He was staring at the construct, which was dissolving back into inert mana. His breathing was ragged. His hands were shaking.

"Wintersbloom," Magister Brimstone said, her voice sharp. "Report."

"I saw something," Flynn said. "When the construct lunged. For a second, I saw something else. Not the construct. Something real. A memory. My aunt — my father's sister — she was a Pyromancer. She died when I was eight. A Calamity killed her. I was there. I was supposed to be in the safe room, but I snuck out to watch, and I saw—"

He stopped. His voice broke.

"I saw it take her. It wasn't a Spawn. It was something bigger. Something with eyes that weren't just wrong, they were... familiar. Like it recognized me. Like it knew who I was. I've never told anyone. Not my father. Not anyone. I buried it. And the construct just brought it back."

The silence in the chamber was absolute.

Magister Brimstone's expression shifted — not to sympathy, not to concern, but to something that Tomas recognized as the particular focus of a combat instructor who had just identified a critical vulnerability. "When was this?"

"Eight years ago. Miravale countryside. My family has a summer estate near the Thornwood border."

"And the Calamity. Describe it."

Flynn's description was fragmented, halting, full of the gaps and distortions of a child's memory. But certain details were sharp: the way the creature had moved, the way it had seemed to know where people were hiding, the way it had whispered — actually whispered, in a language that wasn't language but that Flynn had somehow understood.

"The masters of the Fractured North," Magister Brimstone said quietly. "You saw one of the masters."

The words hit the room like a physical blow. Tomas felt the ram tense beside him — not in alarm, but in recognition. The totem knew that phrase. It had known it all along.

"The masters are real?" Flynn's voice was barely a whisper.

"The masters are real. The Fracture didn't create them — they created the Fracture. Or perhaps the Fracture created them. The scholarship is unclear. What is clear is that they exist, and they remember, and they do not forget the faces of the pact-bound." Magister Brimstone paused. "You saw one of them. It saw you. And if it saw you, it marked you."

Flynn swayed. Tomas caught him before he could fall — the bond between them flaring bright, Tomas's steadiness bleeding into Flynn's panic, the way the ram had taught him to do.

"I marked you too," Tomas said, not knowing where the words came from. "The bond marks both of us. If it comes for you, it comes through me."

"You don't know what you're promising."

"I never know what I'm promising. That's kind of the point."

Flynn stared at him for a long moment. Then, so quietly that only Tomas could hear: "You're an idiot."

"I've been told."

"A complete, impossible idiot."

"And yet you gave me your mana."

Flynn didn't have an answer for that. Neither did Tomas. But the bond between them was steadier than it had ever been, and the ram was standing guard with an expression that looked, impossibly, like satisfaction.

---

That night, the Headmaster summoned them both.

Aldric Vowkeeper's office was at the very top of the Spire, in a tower room that had no visible stairs. You reached it by standing in the center of the Grand Hall and speaking your name — the campus took care of the rest. Tomas had never been in the tower before. Flynn, as a Wintersbloom, had visited twice as a child, but the memory was faded and his father had done most of the talking.

The office was smaller than Tomas had expected. A single room, circular, with windows on all sides showing the campus from above. Bookshelves lined the walls — not the dusty, crowded shelves of Magister Thorne's office, but orderly ranks of volumes bound in the colors of the seven Divisions. At the center was a desk of pale wood, and behind the desk sat Headmaster Vowkeeper, looking exactly as he had during the Pact-Planting: old, patient, and deeply, quietly watchful.

"Sit," he said.

They sat.

"We have a problem," the Headmaster said. "Several problems, actually. The provisional combat pact you formed in the field is, as Magister Brimstone has undoubtedly informed you, extraordinarily rare. The campus has recorded three such bonds in its entire history. Each one preceded a period of significant instability in the pact system. The bonds themselves were not the cause — they were the response. The world was becoming more dangerous, and the campus responded by forging stronger protectors."

He paused, looking from Tomas to Flynn and back.

"The Fracture Line has been pulsing more brightly this term than in any of the past fifty years. The Calamity incursion during your field exercise was the first Spawn sighting within PactSpire's boundary zone in twelve years. And now, Magister Brimstone tells me that one of you —" He looked at Flynn. "— may have been marked by a master Calamity as a child. These are not coincidences."

"The world is becoming more dangerous," Tomas said.

"Yes."

"And we're the response."

"It appears so." Headmaster Vowkeeper leaned back in his chair. "I will not lie to you. The path ahead is not going to be easy. You will face challenges that first-years should not have to face. You will be asked to grow faster than is safe or comfortable. And you will have to rely on each other in ways that go far beyond what any training drill can simulate."

"We already rely on each other," Flynn said.

"You do. But trust forged in crisis is like steel that has been heated but not tempered. It is strong, but it can shatter. The tempering process takes time. It takes repeated stress, in controlled conditions, until the trust is not just a response to danger but a habit of the soul." The Headmaster's eyes met theirs, one after the other. "The Masters will be difficult with you. The campus will arrange things to test you. I am telling you this now so that you understand: it is not punishment. It is preparation."

"For what?"

"The next Calamity War." Headmaster Vowkeeper's voice was very quiet. "It began fifteen years ago. The Fractured North has been expanding at a rate not seen since the Oath-Breaking. The Guilds have been hiding the data, hoping the trend would reverse. It has not. By the time you two graduate, the kingdoms will be at war. My hope — my desperate, calculated hope — is that when that moment comes, there will be enough people like you to meet it."

The silence that followed was the heaviest Tomas had ever felt.

"How long do we have?" he asked.

"Three years. Perhaps four. The Masters of the Fractured North are patient, but they are not idle. And the mark on Flynn means they know who we are training." Headmaster Vowkeeper stood. "Go. Train. Learn. Trust each other. And when the time comes, be ready."

---

They walked back to the Dormitory Ring in silence. The campus's night-cycle was in full effect — hallways soft-lit, stones warm, the distant sound of wind chimes that had been installed by some long-ago Stage Division student and never removed.

At the junction where Hall Verdant split from Hall Clarion, Flynn stopped.

"I'm sorry," he said.

"For what?"

"For dragging you into this. My mark. My memory. My—" He gestured vaguely. "—everything. You didn't sign up for a Calamity War. You signed up for Combat Fundamentals."

"I didn't sign up for anything," Tomas said. "The ram showed up. I followed it here. That's the whole story."

"That's not the whole story."

"No. But it's the part that matters." Tomas looked at Flynn, and the bond between them was quiet but present — a steady hum, like the warmth of the campus floor. "I don't know what I'm doing. I don't know if I'm going to survive this, or if you are, or if any of us will. But I know that when the Spawn came at me, you gave me your mana without thinking. You didn't calculate. You didn't perform. You just gave. And that means something."

"What does it mean?"

"I don't know yet. But I think we're going to find out."

Flynn looked at him for a long moment. Then, without a word, he turned and walked toward Hall Clarion. Tomas watched him go. The ram, which had been silent throughout the entire conversation, made a sound that was almost like a question.

"I don't know either," Tomas said to it. "But we're going to find out."

He walked into Hall Verdant, and the campus — patient, ancient, endlessly observing — dimmed its lights and settled into silence.
