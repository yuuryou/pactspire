# Chapter Eight: The Alchemical-Stage Duel

The challenge came in the form of a letter.

Flynn found it pinned to his seat in the Pact Theory lecture hall — a folded square of parchment, sealed with the crest of the Apothecarian Guild, addressed in a hand he recognized. His father's writing. But the content was not from his father.

*To the Cantrip Jester who dishonors the Wintersbloom name:*

*You are invited to participate in an Alchemical-Stage Duel, to be held on the second Saturday of autumn term, in the Grand Hall. The challenge is issued by Gryphon Vale, Journeyman Apothecarian of Miravale Academy, on behalf of the Apothecarian Guild of Miravale. The format will be traditional: three rounds of alchemical argumentation, judged by impartial Masters. The stakes: public acknowledgment of the superiority of Apothecarian discipline over Stage frivolity, or public acknowledgment of the equal standing of Cantrip Jester within the Wintersbloom line.*

*Refusal will be considered acknowledgment by default.*

*Signed and witnessed,*
*Gryphon Vale, J.A.M.A.*

Flynn read the letter three times, his hands perfectly steady, his expression perfectly blank. Around him, the other students were taking their seats for the lecture. Renn was examining a practice blade. Sera was reviewing her notes. Kael was feeding Pip a strip of dried meat. No one had noticed.

Tomas noticed.

The bond between them had grown sensitive over the past month — not intrusive, not constant, but present. A background hum that spiked when one of them was distressed. Tomas looked across the lecture hall and saw Flynn holding the letter, and the ram tensed beside him.

"What happened?" Tomas said, sliding into the seat beside Flynn.

"Nothing."

"That's not nothing."

Flynn handed him the letter. Tomas read it. The ram read it — or seemed to, its head tilted, its expression darkening.

"Who's Gryphon Vale?"

"Nobody. A second-tier Apothecarian from my father's guild. He was my father's apprentice for three years. He used to call me 'the heir apparent' and bring me candied ginger from the Miravale markets." Flynn's voice was perfectly controlled. "Now he's calling me a dishonor and challenging me to a formal duel."

"Can you win?"

"I don't know. Alchemical-Stage duels aren't about combat. They're about argumentation. Alchemical theory versus Stage improvisation. Each duelist presents a problem, and the other has to solve it using their Class discipline. Gryphon is a Journeyman. I'm a first-year Apprentice. He's been studying alchemical theory since before I was born." Flynn folded the letter with precise, angry creases. "It's not a challenge. It's an execution. My father wants me to fail publicly so I'll come home."

"You don't know that."

"Yes, I do."

Tomas didn't argue. The ram didn't argue. They had both learned, over the weeks, when Flynn's certainty was defensiveness and when it was genuine knowledge. This was genuine.

"Then we'll make sure you don't fail," Tomas said.

Flynn looked at him. "We?"

"The cohort. All of us. You're not doing this alone."

---

The cohort met that evening in the common room of Hall Verdant.

The autumn term had brought new challenges — more advanced Combat Fundamentals drills, a new cultivation technique that Magister Willowmere assured them would be "unpleasant but necessary," and the first formal assessments of their academic progress — but the challenge letter had taken priority over all of it. Kael had spread the news with the efficiency of someone who considered gossip a form of aerial reconnaissance. Sera had arrived with a stack of books on alchemical theory that she had checked out of the Pact Library before Flynn had even asked. Ivy had her rune-etched notebook open, already cataloging potential argumentation strategies. Wren was in their corner, watching, saying nothing.

Even Renn had set aside his evening drill practice. "A formal challenge from a Journeyman to a first-year Apprentice is not just disrespectful," he said. "It's dishonorable. The Drakengard way would be to challenge Vale directly for issuing a challenge with unequal standing."

"Can you do that?" Kael asked.

"Yes. But it wouldn't help. Vale's challenge is to Flynn, not to me. If Flynn doesn't answer, he's acknowledged defeat. That's how these duels work."

"Then Flynn answers," Tomas said.

Everyone looked at him.

"Flynn answers, and Flynn wins, and the Apothecarian Guild has to publicly acknowledge that a Cantrip Jester is not 'Stage frivolity' but a legitimate Class discipline." Tomas's voice was quiet, the way it always was when he was sure of something. "That's the only way this ends. Not with someone else fighting his battles. With him fighting his own and winning."

Flynn was silent. His Quick-Cast was sparking — the unconscious leakage of a Jester under stress — and his hands were clasped in his lap to hide the tremor.

"I don't know if I can win," he said.

"Neither does Gryphon Vale," Tomas said. "He thinks he's going to win. He thinks it's going to be easy. People who think it's going to be easy make mistakes."

"That's not a strategy."

"No. It's a weakness. We find the weakness, and we build the strategy around it."

Ivy looked up from her notebook. "Gryphon Vale is a Journeyman Apothecarian. That means he's completed at least four years of formal training at Miravale Academy, passed the Guild certification examinations, and published at least one original alchemical thesis. His specialty, according to the Guild registry, is transmutation theory — specifically, the application of pact-essence to volatile compounds."

"How do you know that?" Kael asked.

"I looked it up. It's public record."

"When did you look it up?"

"During dinner."

Kael stared at her. Pip stared at her. Ivy stared back with the particular patience of someone who considered research to be a basic social courtesy.

"What's his weakness?" Flynn asked.

"The same as every Apothecarian. Alchemical theory requires preparation. An Apothecarian duelist goes into a challenge with a set of known compounds, known reactions, known solutions. They're brilliant at solving problems within their prepared framework. They're terrible at improvisation." Ivy's eyes met Flynn's. "You're a Cantrip Jester. Improvisation is what you do."

"That's not enough. Alchemical argumentation is structured. There are rules. Traditions. You can't just improvise your way through it."

"You can if you know the rules well enough to break them correctly."

Flynn looked at Ivy with an expression that Tomas couldn't quite read. Something between surprise and respect.

"You're helping me," Flynn said.

"You're in my cohort."

"That's never mattered to you before."

"It's always mattered." Ivy closed her notebook with a snap. "I just don't perform it the way other people do."

---

The weeks leading up to the duel were the most intense of Flynn's life.

He trained with the cohort every evening. But during the day, he trained with Ivy — not in Combat Fundamentals, not in mana cultivation, but in alchemical theory. Ivy had, apparently, taken courses in alchemy as part of her Forge Division preparation, and she had absorbed the material with the same methodical thoroughness she brought to everything else.

"You need to understand the framework," she said, spreading a dozen reference texts across the library table. "Alchemical argumentation follows a specific pattern: thesis, reagent, reaction, result. The duke presents a problem. You either solve it using Stage techniques, or you prove it can't be solved using alchemical ones. The key to the second option is understanding alchemical limitations — what can't be done, and why."

"Can't you teach me the first option?"

"I can. But Gryphon Vale has been solving alchemical problems for years. He'll present challenges designed to be beyond Apprentice-level Stage capability. Your best strategy is to disprove the problem's validity rather than trying to solve it."

"That's a defensive strategy."

"I'm a Forge Division student. I always prefer defense."

Flynn studied. He studied until his eyes blurred and his Quick-Cast sputtered and the Pact Library's warmth became oppressive rather than comforting. He studied the properties of volatile compounds and the mathematics of transmutation and the intricate, beautiful, maddening logic of alchemical theory that his father had tried to teach him when he was eight years old and he had refused to learn because he was too busy reading Stage Division histories under his covers by candlelight.

And somewhere in the middle of the studying, he realized something.

He remembered.

Not consciously — not in the way that Apothecarian training was supposed to work, with memorized formulas and drilled procedures. But subliminally. The language of alchemy was still there, buried under years of deliberate forgetting, and as Ivy walked him through the fundamentals, fragments of it began to resurface. The names of compounds. The logic of reactions. The particular rhythm of an alchemical proof.

It had never left him. He had just stopped listening.

"You know more than you think," Tomas said, one evening after the others had left. They were alone in the common room, the fire dying, the ram standing guard by the door. "I can feel it through the bond. Something clicking into place. Something you're remembering."

"I don't want to remember."

"Why not?"

"Because remembering means my father was right. If I'm good at alchemy — if I have a talent for it that I've been pretending doesn't exist — then maybe I should have been an Apothecarian after all. Maybe the Cantrip Jester class was a mistake. Maybe he was right to stop writing."

Tomas looked at him for a long moment. The ram looked at him. The fire crackled.

"My ram is a sheep," Tomas said.

"What?"

"The totem. It's a ram. Everyone keeps calling it a sheep because it looks like a sheep and I'm a shepherd. And I keep correcting them, because a ram is not a sheep, and it matters. But the thing is..." He paused. "Rams are male sheep. They're a type of sheep. I've been correcting something that isn't wrong, because I didn't want to admit that the ram and the flock are connected."

Flynn stared at him. "That's the most absurd metaphor I've ever heard."

"I know. I'm not good at metaphors."

"Then why are you telling me this?"

"Because being good at alchemy doesn't make you an Apothecarian. You can be good at something and still choose something else. The Cantrip Jester class is what you are. Alchemy is just a thing you know. Those aren't the same."

Flynn was silent. The bond between them hummed — a steady, quiet frequency, like the campus floor in winter. Tomas didn't push. He had learned, over the months, that Flynn didn't respond to pushing. He responded to patience. To the kind of steadiness that the ram provided without asking for anything in return.

"I know the compounds," Flynn said quietly. "All of them. The ones my father made me memorize when I was eight. The ones Gryphon Vale will use. I know what they do and how they interact and why they fail. I've known it all along."

"Then you know more than Gryphon Vale thinks you do."

"Yes."

"That's your advantage."

Flynn looked at him — really looked, the way he had started looking at Tomas since the field exercise, as if he was seeing something that had been invisible before.

"Thank you," he said.

"For what?"

"For not performing."

---

The day of the duel arrived grey and cold — the first true autumn weather, the sky low and heavy with clouds that threatened rain but never delivered. The Grand Hall was packed. Not just with students — with visitors. Apothecarian Guild members from Miravale, Masters from PactSpire, representatives from all seven Divisions who had heard about the challenge and come to watch. Gryphon Vale had publicized the event widely, hoping for maximum humiliation. He had gotten it. But the crowd was not entirely on his side.

Flynn walked to the center of the Hall alone. He had asked the cohort to stay back — not from pride, but from strategy. If Gryphon saw Flynn surrounded by support, he would adjust his approach. If he saw Flynn alone, he would underestimate him. And Gryphon Vale, like every Apothecarian Flynn had ever known, was at his most vulnerable when he was feeling superior.

The adjudicator was a senior Apothecarian from Miravale — a neutral party, chosen by the Guild to ensure fairness. He was old, grey-haired, with the particular weariness of someone who had judged a hundred such duels and expected to judge a hundred more.

"Alchemical-Stage Duel," he announced. "Challenger: Gryphon Vale, Journeyman Apothecarian. Challenged: Flynn Wintersbloom, Apprentice Cantrip Jester. Three rounds. Each round, the challer presents an alchemical problem. The challenged must either solve the problem using Stage techniques or disprove the problem's validity within alchemical theory. Victory by majority judgment of the three Masters."

He paused, looking at Flynn with something that might have been sympathy.

"If the challed cannot answer within the time limit, the round is forfeit. If the challed forfeits two rounds, the duel is lost. Are the terms understood?"

"Understood," Gryphon said. He was tall, dark-haired, impeccably dressed in the robes of a Journeyman Apothecarian. His expression was confident — the confidence of someone who had never lost an alchemical argument to a first-year.

"Understood," Flynn said. His expression was blank.

"Round one. Challer, present."

Gryphon stepped forward. The crowd hushed.

"Problem," Gryphon said. "A Pyromancer in the field has sustained a mana-burn equivalent to third-degree thermal damage. Standard Apothecarian treatment requires a poultice of star-flower, moon-thistle, and pact-essence base, applied within six hours of injury. The Pyromancer's unit is twelve hours from the nearest Apothecarian supply point. How do you, as a Cantrip Jester, resolve this?"

The crowd murmured. It was a deliberately unfair problem — a medical scenario designed for a healing Class, presented to a performing Class, with a time constraint that made any Stage solution impractical.

Flynn smiled. It was not a pleasant smile.

"Disprove," he said.

"On what grounds?"

"The problem assumes that star-flower is the only analgesic compound available in the field. It is not. Dusk-root and fire-fern, both common to Thornwood terrain, contain identical active alkaloids and can be harvested and processed without Apothecarian equipment. The Pyromancer's unit travels through Thornwood terrain. They will encounter both plants. A Cantrip Jester cannot manufacture a poultice, but a Cantrip Jester can create a vivid, accurate illusory guide to the plants' appearance and preparation method, allowing any member of the unit to produce the poultice without Apothecarian training."

The crowd went silent.

Gryphon's expression flickered — surprise, quickly suppressed. "You're proposing a workaround, not a solution."

"I'm proposing that your problem was flawed. You presented a scenario with a hidden assumption — that only an Apothecarian can produce Apothecarian results. That assumption is false. Dusk-root and fire-fern are taught in introductory Apothecarian botany. You knew they existed. You chose not to mention them. That is not a problem. That is a trap. And I have just disarmed it."

The three Masters conferred. The Miravale adjudicator — who, Flynn noticed, was looking at Gryphon with an expression that was not entirely pleased — announced: "Round one to the challenged. One point to Wintersbloom."

---

The second round was harder.

Gryphon had been humiliated, and his response was not caution but escalation. He presented a transmutation problem — a volatile compound that could only be stabilized through a specific Apothecarian technique, in a scenario where the Apothecarian was incapacitated and the compound was seconds from explosion.

Flynn listened. His mind moved through the alchemical principles — the catalysis requirements, the stabilization thresholds, the exact temperature at which the compound became unrecoverable. And then, in a flash of intuition that felt more like the ram than like himself, he saw it.

"Disprove," he said.

"On what grounds?"

"The compound you described is fictional."

A ripple went through the crowd. Gryphon's face went very still.

"It is a standard transmutation problem from the Miravale Advanced Alchemy curriculum," Gryphon said. "The compound is known to every Journeyman Apothecarian in the guild."

"Known, but not real. The compound requires a stabilization threshold of 847 degrees, sustained for precisely 2.3 seconds. But alchemical theory states that no volatile compound can be stabilized above 800 degrees without a pact-essence catalyst — and the base alchemical catalyst you described degrades at 750 degrees. Your compound is physically impossible. It exists only as a theoretical exercise, designed to teach students to identify impossibility. You presented it as a practical problem, hoping I wouldn't know the difference."

Silence.

The three Masters conferred for longer this time. One of them — a Stage Division Master, Flynn noticed — was smiling slightly.

"Round two to the challenged," the adjudicator announced, and his voice was different now. Less weary. More interested. "Two points to Wintersbloom. The duel is concluded. Wintersbloom wins."

The crowd erupted.

Not everyone was pleased — the visiting Apothecarians were muttering, the Guild representatives exchanging tight-lipped looks — but the PactSpire students were cheering. Kael was shouting something incoherent from the front row. Pip was screeching. Sera was smiling, the first genuine smile Tomas had ever seen on her face.

And Flynn Wintersbloom stood alone in the center of the Grand Hall, his Quick-Cast sparking violet and gold, his expression caught between triumph and terror, and looked across the room to where Tomas Herdsson was standing with the ram at his side.

Tomas nodded. Once. The same nod Tomas always gave before a difficult drill — the nod that said *you can do this, I know you can do this, now do it*.

Flynn nodded back.

Gryphon Vale approached him after the crowd had begun to disperse. His expression was complicated — anger and humiliation and something that looked almost like respect.

"Your father," Gryphon said quietly, "told me you had no head for alchemy."

"He was wrong."

"Apparently." Gryphon paused. "For what it's worth, I didn't want to issue that challenge. Your father insisted. He said it was the only way to make you see reason."

"Did it work?"

Gryphon looked at Flynn — really looked, the way people were starting to look at him since the Pact-Planting. "No. I think it did the opposite."

"Good."

Gryphon smiled — a thin, reluctant expression. "You're not what I expected, Wintersbloom."

"No one ever is."

---

That night, the cohort celebrated in Hall Verdant.

The fire was high, the food was plentiful, and Kael had somehow procured more Drakengard fire-wine despite having been explicitly told not to. Pip was defending a plate of roast meat. Ivy was taking notes on the duel's strategic implications. Wren was in their corner, watching, and their expression was — for the first time — almost warm.

"You knew the compounds," Ivy said to Flynn. "All of them. You knew dusk-root and fire-fern. You knew the stabilization threshold. At the Apprentice level, that's not possible unless..."

"Unless I was trained as a child," Flynn said. "Yes. My father started my alchemy lessons when I was six. I pretended to forget everything after my awakening. But it was still there. I just had to let myself remember."

"Why didn't you want to remember?"

"Because remembering meant my father was right about something. And if he was right about alchemy, maybe he was right about everything else."

"And now?"

Flynn looked across the room to where Tomas was sitting with the ram, not talking, not performing, just being there in the steady, quiet way that had become as familiar as breathing.

"Now I know he wasn't right about everything," Flynn said. "He was just right about alchemy. That's a much smaller thing."

Ivy nodded. She closed her notebook and, for the first time in the five months Flynn had known her, smiled.

"Good," she said. "Then we can move on to the next problem."

"What problem?"

"The Alchemical-Stage duel proved that Stage improvisation can match Apothecarian preparation. But it didn't prove that Stage improvisation is *better*. There's a theoretical argument to be made for complementary disciplines rather than competitive ones. I've been developing it for weeks. I'd like your input."

Flynn stared at her. "You want my input on an academic argument about my own Class discipline."

"Yes."

"After I just spent three weeks studying alchemy under your direction."

"Yes."

"You're completely impossible, Belladonna."

"I've been told."

And somewhere in the corner, Wren Vesper smiled — a small, private expression that no one saw — and wrote something in their journal that was not for Silas Thorne.
